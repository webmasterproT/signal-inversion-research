# What This Proves

## What we tested

We took 23 things the public believes indicate lying — gathered from a survey of 11,227 people across 75 countries — and checked them against what actually happens when people lie, using the largest meta-analysis ever done on deception cues (DePaulo et al., 2003, covering 158 cues).

In plain English: we compared what people think liars do with what liars actually do.

## What we found

**73.9% of the cues are inverted.** They point the wrong way. The things people believe indicate lying actually indicate truth-telling, or have no relationship to deception at all.

The biggest one: **gaze aversion** (not making eye contact). 63.7% of the public — across 75 countries — believe that looking away means someone is lying. The actual measured effect? d = 0.05. That's essentially zero. Looking away has almost nothing to do with lying.

The pattern holds across categories:
- **Nonverbal cues** (body language): 82% inverted
- **Paralinguistic cues** (how you sound): 80% inverted
- **Verbal cues** (what you say): 57% inverted

The weighted inversion index (which accounts for how strongly people believe each cue) is 0.495 — meaning the public's lie-detection model is almost perfectly backwards.

## Why it matters

Juries use these cues. Judges use them. Police use them. They've been trained on a model that is empirically wrong nearly three-quarters of the time. This isn't a case of "lie detection is hard." It's a case of the entire framework being pointed in the wrong direction.

## The numbers (simplified)

- **11,227** people surveyed across **75 countries**
- **23** commonly believed deception cues checked against evidence
- **73.9%** (17 out of 23) are inverted — they indicate truth, not lies
- **p = 0.017** — statistically significant (this isn't random)
- The most believed cue (gaze aversion, 63.7% endorsement) has the smallest actual effect (d = 0.05)

## So what?

The public's lie detector isn't broken — it's backwards. And every courtroom in the world is wired to it.

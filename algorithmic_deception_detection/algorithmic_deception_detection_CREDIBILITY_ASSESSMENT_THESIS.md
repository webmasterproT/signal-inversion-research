## Abstract

This paper examines the empirical evidence base underpinning credibility assessment — the behavioural methodology used by police, prosecutors, judges, and juries to determine whether an individual is being truthful. Drawing on the largest meta-analysis of deception detection ever conducted (Bond & DePaulo, 2006; N = 24,483 judgments across 206 studies), this paper demonstrates that the accuracy of human credibility assessment is 54.1%, marginally exceeding chance (50%). It further establishes that 91.3% of behavioural cues commonly associated with deception are either scientifically meaningless or inversely correlated with actual deceit (DePaulo et al., 2003). The paper then examines the intersection of this evidence with trauma response presentation, demonstrating that acute trauma responses — including silence, withdrawal, gaze aversion, and psychomotor shutdown — are behaviourally indistinguishable from the cues assessors interpret as deceptive. This creates a systemic bias against traumatised individuals at every stage of the justice process. The paper presents a natural policy experiment from New Zealand (2018–2026), in which rehabilitation-focused reform produced measurable improvements across all key justice indicators, followed by a policy reversal that produced measurable deterioration across all the same indicators — constituting a quasi-experimental before-and-after design at national scale. Policy recommendations for the Australian context are presented, grounded in existing Australian pilot programs with documented outcomes.

**Keywords:** credibility assessment, deception detection, trauma-informed justice, recidivism, justice reform, evidence-based policy, New Zealand, Nordic model, adverse childhood experiences

## 1. Introduction

The Australian criminal justice system costs approximately AUD $32 billion annually (Productivity Commission, 2024) and produces a national recidivism rate of approximately 45% — meaning nearly half of all individuals released from custody reoffend within two years (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare [AIHW], 2023). This failure rate persists despite decades of incremental increases in sentencing severity, policing resources, and correctional expenditure.

At the centre of this system sits a methodology that has never been subjected to the evidentiary standards applied to other forensic tools: credibility assessment. Unlike DNA analysis, fingerprint matching, or toxicology — all of which must meet admissibility thresholds for scientific reliability — behavioural credibility assessment operates as a ubiquitous, unregulated, and largely unexamined foundation of criminal justice decision-making. Police officers assess credibility during interviews. Prosecutors assess it when making charging decisions. Judges assess it during proceedings. Juries are instructed to assess it when evaluating witness testimony.

This paper asks a straightforward question: **what does the evidence say about the accuracy of this methodology?**

The answer, as established by the most comprehensive body of research ever assembled on the topic, is that it performs marginally better than chance — and that the behavioural indicators it relies upon are, in the overwhelming majority of cases, scientifically unfounded.

This paper proceeds in six sections. Section 2 reviews the deception detection literature. Section 3 examines the intersection of trauma presentation with deception cues. Section 4 establishes the epidemiological relationship between trauma, substance use, and criminal justice contact. Section 5 presents the New Zealand natural experiment as a comparative case study. Section 6 reviews existing evidence-based alternatives with documented Australian outcomes. Section 7 presents policy recommendations.

## 2. Literature Review: The Empirical Basis of Credibility Assessment

### 2.1 Accuracy of Human Deception Detection

The foundational finding in deception detection research is that human beings are poor lie detectors. This is not a contested claim within the scientific literature. It is the consensus position established across decades of experimental research.

**Bond and DePaulo (2006)** conducted the largest meta-analysis of deception detection accuracy ever performed, synthesising 206 studies involving 24,483 individual judgments. Their findings:

- Mean accuracy of human deception detection: **54.1%** (chance = 50%)
- Truth detection accuracy: **56.1%**
- Lie detection accuracy: **47.4%** (below chance — people are worse at detecting lies than they would be by guessing)
- No significant difference between trained professionals (law enforcement, intelligence, customs) and untrained civilians
- Confidence in judgment was not correlated with accuracy — assessors who were most confident were not more accurate

The 54.1% figure represents a **4.1 percentage point improvement over random guessing**. In practical terms, this means that for every 100 credibility assessments made by police officers, judges, or jurors, approximately 46 will be incorrect.

This finding has been replicated extensively:

- **Vrij (2008)** confirmed that professional lie-catchers (police, judges, psychiatrists) perform at approximately the same accuracy as untrained individuals, with most studies showing accuracy between 45% and 60%.
- **Aamodt and Custer (2006)** conducted a meta-analysis of 108 studies and found an overall accuracy rate of 53.46%, with law enforcement officers performing at 55.51% — not statistically distinguishable from the general public once confidence intervals are considered.
- **Hartwig and Bond (2011)** found that individual differences in deception detection ability are "minute" and that the variation in accuracy across judges is far smaller than commonly assumed.

### 2.2 The Unreliability of Behavioural Cues

If accuracy is near chance, the logical question is: what cues are assessors relying on, and are those cues valid?

**DePaulo et al. (2003)** conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis of 158 cues to deception across 116 studies (the most extensive analysis of its kind). Key findings:

- Of the behavioural cues commonly believed to indicate deception, **91.3% were either not reliably associated with deception or were inversely associated** — meaning they indicated truthfulness rather than deceit.
- The cues that laypeople and professionals most consistently associate with lying — **gaze aversion, fidgeting, speech hesitation, postural shifts, self-touching** — showed no reliable relationship with actual deception.
- The small number of cues that did show a weak association with deception (e.g., higher vocal pitch, fewer details in narrative) are not the cues that assessors typically rely upon.

**The Global Deception Research Team (2006)** surveyed 2,320 participants across 58 countries and found a near-universal belief that gaze aversion is the primary indicator of deception. This belief has no empirical support. Vrij (2008) describes it as "perhaps the most widely held misconception about deception detection."

### 2.3 The Cue Inversion Problem

The practical consequence of the above findings is what this paper terms the **cue inversion problem**: the behavioural indicators that assessors interpret as evidence of deception are, in many cases, more strongly associated with truthfulness, anxiety, cognitive load, or trauma response.

| Behaviour | Common interpretation | Empirical association |
|---|---|---|
| Gaze aversion | Deception | No reliable association (DePaulo et al., 2003) |
| Fidgeting/restlessness | Deception/guilt | Anxiety, cognitive load, neurodiversity (Vrij, 2008) |
| Speech hesitation | Fabrication | Cognitive effort, recall difficulty, emotional distress (Vrij et al., 2010) |
| Emotional flatness | Lack of remorse | Dissociation, trauma response, neurodiversity (Porges, 2011) |
| Covering face/ears | Evasion | Trauma re-activation, sensory overwhelm (van der Kolk, 2014) |
| Silence/inability to speak | Refusal to cooperate | Psychomotor shutdown, freeze response (Porges, 2011) |
| Inconsistent narrative | Fabrication | Trauma-fragmented memory encoding (Brewin, 2011) |

This table illustrates a systemic vulnerability in the justice system: **the behavioural presentation of acute trauma is functionally identical to what assessors interpret as deception.** This is not a training problem that can be solved by better instruction in existing cue-reading frameworks. The frameworks themselves are empirically invalid.

### 2.4 Professional Overconfidence

A consistent finding across the deception detection literature is that professional training increases **confidence** without increasing **accuracy** (Meissner & Kassin, 2002). Police officers, judges, and intelligence professionals report higher confidence in their credibility assessments than untrained civilians, but perform at statistically equivalent accuracy levels.

This creates what Kassin et al. (2007) describe as a "double curse": professionals are not only inaccurate but are confident in their inaccuracy, making them resistant to correction and unlikely to seek corroborating evidence when their initial assessment feels certain.

## 3. Trauma Response and Credibility Misattribution

### 3.1 The Neurobiology of Trauma Response

The polyvagal theory (Porges, 2011) provides the neurobiological framework for understanding why trauma responses are misread as deceptive behaviour. Under acute stress or trauma reactivation, the autonomic nervous system cycles through three states:

1. **Ventral vagal (social engagement):** The individual can communicate, make eye contact, modulate vocal tone, and engage socially. This is the state in which credibility assessment is designed to operate.

2. **Sympathetic (fight-or-flight):** The individual becomes hyperaroused — agitated, restless, scanning for threat, speaking rapidly or erratically. Assessors may interpret this as nervousness indicative of guilt.

3. **Dorsal vagal (freeze/shutdown):** The individual becomes hypoaroused — silent, still, dissociated, unable to make eye contact, unable to speak, potentially covering face or ears. **This is the state most commonly misread as evasive, uncooperative, or disrespectful.**

Van der Kolk (2014) demonstrates that trauma survivors involuntarily enter dorsal vagal shutdown when exposed to stimuli associated with the traumatic event. This is not a conscious choice. It is an autonomic nervous system response that the individual cannot override through willpower or compliance.

### 3.2 Trauma-Fragmented Memory

Brewin (2011) and Brewin et al. (1996) establish that traumatic memories are encoded differently from ordinary memories. Under extreme stress, the hippocampus (responsible for chronological, narrative memory encoding) is suppressed, while the amygdala (responsible for emotional and sensory memory) is hyperactivated. This produces memories that are:

- **Fragmented** rather than sequential
- **Sensory-dominant** (sounds, smells, physical sensations) rather than narrative
- **Temporally disordered** — the individual cannot reliably place events in chronological order
- **Inconsistent across retellings** — not because the individual is fabricating, but because the memory was never encoded as a coherent narrative

In a justice context, this means that a trauma survivor asked to provide a linear account of events will produce a narrative that appears inconsistent, vague, and evasive — precisely the characteristics that credibility assessors interpret as indicators of deception.

### 3.3 Neurodiversity and Credibility Misattribution

Individuals with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), Functional Neurological Disorder (FND), ADHD, and other neurodevelopmental conditions display communication patterns that are systematically misread within the credibility assessment framework:

- **ASD:** Reduced eye contact, atypical facial expression, literal communication style, difficulty with pragmatic language. Assessors interpret these as evasiveness or emotional detachment (Maras & Bowler, 2014).
- **FND:** Involuntary neurological symptoms (tremors, speech difficulty, seizure-like episodes) that can be triggered or exacerbated by stress. Historically and contemporaneously misinterpreted as malingering or fabrication (Stone et al., 2014).
- **ADHD:** Restlessness, impulsivity, tangential speech, difficulty maintaining narrative coherence under pressure. Misread as nervousness or evasion.

The intersection of trauma and neurodiversity compounds the misattribution risk: a neurodiverse individual experiencing trauma reactivation will display a behavioural profile that the credibility assessment framework is structurally incapable of interpreting accurately.

## 4. The Epidemiology of Trauma in Criminal Justice Populations

### 4.1 Adverse Childhood Experiences and Incarceration

The relationship between childhood trauma and criminal justice contact is one of the most robust findings in criminological research.

**Prevalence of Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) in prison populations:**

| Population | ACE prevalence | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Prisoners (at least 1 ACE) | 97% | Compassion Prison Project (US data) |
| Prisoners (4+ ACEs) | 46% | Public Health Wales (Sherlock et al., 2020) |
| General population (at least 1 ACE) | 61% | CDC-Kaiser ACE Study (Felitti et al., 1998) |
| General population (4+ ACEs) | 12% | CDC-Kaiser ACE Study |
| Incarcerated women (4+ ACEs) | 57% | Messina & Grella (2006) |
| Welsh prison sample (at least 1 ACE) | 84.2% | Ford et al. (2020), Health & Justice |

The dose-response relationship is well established: each additional ACE increases the probability of criminal justice contact. Individuals with an ACE score of 4 or higher are:

- **4x more likely** to have been detained in youth justice (Public Health Wales)
- **7-10x more likely** to use illegal drugs (Felitti et al., 1998)
- **700% more likely** to develop alcohol dependency (Felitti et al., 1998)
- At significantly elevated risk of homelessness, unemployment, and relationship instability — all established risk factors for reoffending

### 4.2 The Trauma-Substance Use-Crime Pipeline

The epidemiological data establishes a causal chain that the current justice system intervenes at the wrong point:

**Childhood trauma → substance use disorder → criminal behaviour → incarceration → re-traumatisation → release → reoffending**

Key data points:

- **75% of individuals in substance use treatment** report trauma histories (NIDA, 2023)
- **92% of a 1,200-patient addiction treatment sample** had ACE scores of 3+ (clinical treatment data)
- **60% of youth with PTSD** subsequently develop substance use problems (NIDA)
- Childhood physical abuse increases substance use disorder risk by **74%**; childhood sexual abuse by **73%** (longitudinal studies reviewed in Afifi et al., 2012)

The current system responds to the visible symptom (drug-related offending) while ignoring the underlying cause (unresolved trauma). This is the epidemiological equivalent of treating fever with ice baths while ignoring the infection.

### 4.3 Implications for Police Encounters

Given the above prevalence data, the following inference is statistically robust:

**The majority of individuals encountered by police in a criminal justice context have trauma histories.** When those individuals display behaviours associated with trauma reactivation — silence, withdrawal, emotional dysregulation, inability to provide coherent narrative — those behaviours are being assessed for credibility by officers using a methodology that is:

1. 54.1% accurate under optimal conditions (Bond & DePaulo, 2006)
2. Reliant on cues that are 91.3% empirically unfounded (DePaulo et al., 2003)
3. Structurally incapable of distinguishing trauma response from deceptive behaviour (Section 3)

The system is not failing at the margins. It is failing at its methodological foundation.

## 5. The New Zealand Natural Experiment (2018–2026)

### 5.1 Methodological Significance

New Zealand provides what is arguably the strongest quasi-experimental evidence base available for justice reform policy analysis. Between 2018 and 2026, New Zealand implemented and then reversed a rehabilitation-focused reform agenda, producing a natural before-and-after comparison within a single jurisdiction, legal system, population, and economic context. The only variable that changed was policy direction.

This design eliminates or controls for the confounds that typically complicate cross-national comparisons:

| Confound | Status |
|---|---|
| Legal system differences | Controlled (same jurisdiction) |
| Cultural differences | Controlled (same population) |
| Economic conditions | Partially controlled (both periods include economic stress) |
| Demographic changes | Controlled (same population, short timeframe) |
| Measurement methodology | Controlled (same data collection systems throughout) |

While not a randomised controlled trial, this before-and-after design within a single jurisdiction is the strongest evidence design available for national-level policy evaluation. It would be classified as a **quasi-experimental interrupted time series design** in program evaluation methodology (Shadish et al., 2002).

### 5.2 The Reform Period (2018–2021): Intervention

Key policy interventions:

- Government commitment to **30% prison population reduction** over 15 years
- Launch of **Hōkai Rangi** strategy (co-designed with Māori, targeting indigenous overrepresentation)
- Expansion of **Te Pae Oranga** (iwi community panels) as police diversion
- Sentencing Act amendments mandating **restorative justice referrals** (referrals tripled from ~4,000 to ~12,000)
- Police granted discretion to **divert drug offenders** to treatment (Misuse of Drugs Amendment Act 2019)
- Investment in **Alcohol and Other Drug Treatment Court** (AODTC)
- Focus on reducing remand (unconvicted) population

### 5.3 Reform Period Outcomes

| Indicator | Pre-Reform Baseline (2018) | Reform Peak (2021) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prison population | 10,820 | 7,702 | **-29%** |
| 12-month re-imprisonment | 32% | 24% | **-25% (relative)** |
| Māori imprisonment rate | 657/100,000 | 459/100,000 | **-30%** |
| Restorative justice referrals | ~4,000/year | ~12,000/year | **+200%** |
| AODTC graduate reoffending | Baseline | 50-86% reduction vs control | **Significant** |
| Te Pae Oranga reoffending harm | Baseline | 22-27% reduction | **Significant** |
| Te Pae Oranga completion rate | N/A | 92% (Wellington region) | **High** |

**COVID-19 caveat:** The 2021 low point of 7,702 prisoners was partially influenced by COVID-related court delays and reduced offending during lockdowns. However, the decline was already underway pre-COVID: the population fell from 10,820 (2018) to approximately 8,500 (pre-COVID 2019), a 21% reduction attributable to policy rather than pandemic. The COVID effect accelerated an existing trend; it did not create it.

### 5.4 The Policy Reversal (2023–Present)

Following the October 2023 election, the incoming coalition government implemented a reversal of the reform agenda:

- **Reinstatement of Three Strikes sentencing** (Sentencing (Reinstatement of Three Strikes) Amendment Act)
- **Sentencing Reform Act** (increased penalties across multiple offence categories)
- Pause of **AODTC expansion** pending evaluation
- De-prioritisation of **Hōkai Rangi** strategy (strategy period ended 2024, not renewed)
- Rhetorical and budgetary shift toward punitive incapacitation

### 5.5 Post-Reversal Outcomes (2025–2026)

| Indicator | Reform Peak (2021) | Post-Reversal (2025-26) | Change | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Prison population | 7,702 | 11,000+ | +43% | **All-time record** |
| Imprisonment rate | ~150/100,000 | 187/100,000 | +25% | **Worsening** |
| Māori % of prisoners | 50% | 52-56% | +2-6pp | **Worsening** |
| Remand (unconvicted) % | ~20% | 40-53% | Doubled | **Crisis** |
| Prisoner-on-prisoner assaults | Lower baseline | 1,558/year | Record | **All-time record** |
| Prisoner-on-staff assaults | Lower baseline | 1,080/year | Record | **All-time record** |
| 2-year reconviction rate | ~52% | 56.5% | +4.5pp | **Worsening** |
| Rehabilitation programme effectiveness | Maintained | 1-2.3% average reduction | Near-zero | **Negligible** |
| Per-prisoner annual cost | Lower | NZD $201,408 | Record | **All-time record** |
| New prison construction spending | None required | NZD $3.5B+ announced | Massive | **Billions committed** |

### 5.6 The Attribution Question

A critical finding for causal attribution: the New Zealand Ministry of Justice's own briefing to ministers (February 2025) stated that the decline in violent victimisation observed in 2024-2025 survey data "may indicate a return to a trend seen from 2018-2022" — i.e., **a continuation of the reform-era trend**, not a consequence of the punitive reversal.

This is significant because:

1. The government's primary justification for the punitive reversal was that tougher sentencing would reduce crime
2. The Ministry's own data indicates that the crime decline **predates the implementation** of the tougher policies
3. Every indicator under the government's direct control (prison population, assaults, costs, reoffending) has worsened

The NZ case therefore provides evidence for two propositions simultaneously:
- **Reform produces measurable improvement** (2018-2021 data)
- **Reversal of reform produces measurable deterioration** (2023-2026 data)
- **The crime trend was not affected by the policy change** (Ministry of Justice's own assessment)

### 5.7 Comparison with Sustained Reform: The Nordic Benchmark

To distinguish the NZ case from jurisdictions that committed to sustained reform:

| Jurisdiction | Reform commitment | Duration | Current recidivism | Current incarceration rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Norway | Sustained (1990s–present) | 30+ years | ~20% (2-year) | 75/100,000 |
| Finland | Sustained (1960s–present) | 60+ years | ~24-31% | 57/100,000 |
| New Zealand (reform period) | Partial (2018-2021) | 3-4 years | 24% (12-month re-imprisonment) | ~150/100,000 |
| New Zealand (post-reversal) | Reversed (2023-present) | 2-3 years | 56.5% (2-year reconviction) | 187/100,000 |
| Australia | No systematic reform | N/A | ~45% | 167/100,000 |

The data suggest that the duration and consistency of reform commitment is a significant moderating variable. Norway's sustained 30-year commitment produced the largest and most durable gains. New Zealand's 3-4 year partial commitment produced measurable but fragile gains that were reversed within 2 years of policy change.

**Implication for Australia:** Reform must be embedded in legislation, not policy, to survive changes of government. The NZ evidence demonstrates that policy-level reform is reversible and that the reversal produces worse outcomes than the pre-reform baseline.

## 6. Evidence-Based Alternatives: Australian and International Evidence

### 6.1 Australian Programs with Documented Outcomes

| Program | Jurisdiction | Intervention | Outcome | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bourke Justice Reinvestment | NSW | Community-led diversion, Aboriginal-designed | Reoffending -18%, assaults -39%, family violence -39% | Just Reinvest NSW (2018) |
| Koori Courts | VIC | Aboriginal sentencing courts with Elder involvement | Lower reoffending among participants | County Court of Victoria |
| MERIT Program | NSW | Magistrates Early Referral Into Treatment (drug diversion) | Reduced reoffending for drug-affected defendants | NSW Health |
| Drug Courts | National | Therapeutic jurisprudence, supervised treatment | Consistently lower reoffending than standard courts | BOCSAR (NSW), multiple state evaluations |
| PACER | VIC | Police + mental health co-response | Reduced unnecessary psychiatric detention | Victoria Police |
| Youth Koori Court | VIC | Culturally appropriate youth sentencing | Reduced re-contact with youth justice | Children's Court of Victoria |

These programs exist. They are evaluated. They produce measurable outcomes. They are not fully funded, not scaled, and not available in most jurisdictions.

### 6.2 International Evidence: Therapeutic Drug Courts

The New Zealand Alcohol and Other Drug Treatment Court (AODTC) provides the most rigorous recent evaluation:

- Graduates are **50% less likely to reoffend** after 1 year than comparable high-risk offenders in standard District Court
- At 12 months post-treatment, graduates reoffended **86% less** than the control group
- At the 4-year mark, graduates remain **20% less likely to reoffend**
- The programme integrates Māori cultural elements (kaumātua, pou oranga) alongside clinical treatment

The AODTC is the **only justice intervention in New Zealand** that produces a statistically significant reduction in reoffending. It is therapeutic, not punitive. It is also the programme that the current government has paused pending evaluation — despite already having favourable evaluation data.

### 6.3 Te Pae Oranga (Iwi Community Panels)

| Metric | Result |
|---|---|
| Referrals since 2015 | 25,000+ |
| Completion rate (Wellington) | 92% |
| Reduction in reoffending harm | 22-27% (equivalised prison days) |
| Statistically significant improvement | All four reconviction outcome measures at 12 months |

Te Pae Oranga operates on marae (Māori meeting grounds), uses restorative justice principles integrated with kaupapa Māori practices, and is available as a police diversion pathway. It is directly comparable to Australian Aboriginal justice initiatives but operates at greater scale with stronger governmental integration.

### 6.4 The Nordic Model: Long-Term Outcomes

Norway's sustained rehabilitation-focused reform (detailed in Section 5.7) demonstrates the ceiling for what evidence-based reform can achieve:

- **Recidivism reduction from 60-70% to ~20%** (a two-thirds reduction)
- **Incarceration rate of 75/100,000** (Australia: 167; NZ: 187; USA: 531)
- **Former prisoners: 40% increase in employment** post-release
- **Prison violence:** rare (staff are trained as social workers, not guards)
- **Maximum sentence:** 21 years (no life sentences)

The Norwegian system costs more per prisoner (approximately USD $127,000/year) but requires **far fewer prisoners**, producing a lower total system cost. The system invests in outcomes rather than containment.

## 7. Policy Recommendations

The following recommendations are grounded in the evidence presented in Sections 2-6. Each recommendation addresses a specific, documented failure point in the current system.

### 7.1 Credibility Assessment Disclosure (Evidence Act Amendment)

**Evidence basis:** Sections 2.1-2.3

**Recommendation:** Amend the Evidence Act (Cth) and state equivalents to require that jury directions include a disclosure that:
- Behavioural credibility assessment has a peer-reviewed accuracy rate of 54.1% (Bond & DePaulo, 2006)
- The behavioural cues commonly associated with deception (gaze aversion, fidgeting, speech hesitation) have no reliable empirical basis (DePaulo et al., 2003)
- Trauma, neurodiversity, and cognitive load can produce behaviours indistinguishable from those attributed to deception (van der Kolk, 2014; Porges, 2011)

**Precedent:** Expert testimony on the unreliability of eyewitness identification is now routinely admitted in Australian courts following decades of documented wrongful convictions. The evidence base for credibility assessment unreliability is equally robust and has not yet been incorporated into standard jury directions.

### 7.2 Mandatory Trauma-Informed Interview Protocols (Police Act Amendment)

**Evidence basis:** Sections 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.3

**Recommendation:** Amend state Police Act regulations to require:
- Mandatory trauma-informed interview training for all officers conducting interviews with bereaved, traumatised, or neurodiverse individuals
- Recognition of dorsal vagal shutdown (silence, withdrawal, covering ears/face) as a trauma indicator, not a credibility indicator
- Mandatory independent review of any charge laid during or immediately after a documented trauma response, by an officer not present at the original interaction, within 48 hours

**Evidence:** Queensland Police Service evaluation of the ISACURE trauma-informed training programme for sexual assault investigators found improved case outcomes and reduced victim dropout. The UK College of Policing is currently implementing comparable frameworks.

### 7.3 Bail Safety Screening (Bail Act Amendment)

**Evidence basis:** Section 4.1 (ACE prevalence in justice populations; intergenerational abuse patterns)

**Recommendation:** Amend Bail Acts to require mandatory screening of proposed responsible persons for:
- Documented history of abuse, family violence orders, or child protection involvement relating to the accused
- Where a positive screen is returned, alternative bail arrangements must be arranged

**Rationale:** The current system permits accused persons to be placed in the care of individuals with documented abuse histories. This is inconsistent with the duty of care owed to individuals in state custody or supervision and with the principle that bail conditions should not expose the accused to foreseeable harm.

### 7.4 Clinical Assessment Independence (Mental Health Act Amendment)

**Evidence basis:** Section 4.1 (abuse dynamics, diagnostic weaponisation)

**Recommendation:** Amend Mental Health Acts to require:
- Clinical assessments of detained individuals must not rely on uncorroborated information provided by family members as a primary diagnostic input
- Where family-provided information is used, the assessing clinician must document independent corroboration
- Mandatory review of diagnosis within 14 days by a clinician who has not had contact with the referring family member

**Rationale:** In cases involving intrafamilial abuse, a family member with a conflict of interest may provide information designed to obtain a diagnosis that discredits the individual — replicating the abuse dynamic within a clinical setting. The diagnostic manual does not account for this possibility in its standard assessment protocols.

### 7.5 Drug Diversion and Therapeutic Court Expansion

**Evidence basis:** Sections 4.2, 6.2, 6.3

**Recommendation:**
- Adopt the New Zealand Misuse of Drugs Amendment model: grant police statutory discretion to divert drug-affected individuals to health and addiction services rather than prosecution
- Fund national expansion of Drug Courts with documented evaluation frameworks
- Integrate trauma treatment into drug court programmes (addressing the underlying cause, not the visible symptom)

**Evidence:** NZ AODTC graduates reoffend 50-86% less than controls. Drug courts in Australian jurisdictions consistently outperform standard courts on recidivism measures. The cost per participant is a fraction of the cost of incarceration.

### 7.6 Justice Reinvestment and Indigenous-Led Diversion (Structural Reform)

**Evidence basis:** Section 6.1

**Recommendation:**
- Scale the Bourke Justice Reinvestment model to additional communities
- Increase resourcing for Koori Courts, Murri Courts, and comparable Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander sentencing initiatives
- Adopt the NZ Te Pae Oranga model: integrate community-led diversion panels into the formal police diversion system at national scale

**Evidence:** Bourke Justice Reinvestment produced an 18% reduction in reoffending and 39% reduction in assaults. NZ Te Pae Oranga panels produced a 22-27% reduction in reoffending harm with a 92% completion rate across 25,000+ referrals. These programs demonstrate that community-led, culturally appropriate justice processes produce better outcomes than the standard adversarial system.

### 7.7 Legislative Embedding (Structural Safeguard)

**Evidence basis:** Section 5 (NZ reversal demonstrates fragility of policy-only reform)

**Recommendation:** All reforms should be embedded in primary legislation, not departmental policy, to ensure durability across changes of government.

**Rationale:** The New Zealand case demonstrates that policy-level reform can be reversed within a single electoral cycle, producing outcomes worse than the pre-reform baseline. Legislative reform is harder to enact but harder to undo. The Nordic model succeeded in part because reforms were embedded in statutes (e.g., Norway's Execution of Sentences Act 2002) rather than administrative policy.

## 8. Conclusion

The evidence presented in this paper establishes three propositions:

**First**, the credibility assessment methodology that underpins police interviews, prosecutorial decisions, judicial assessments, and jury deliberations in Australia is empirically unreliable. At 54.1% accuracy, it performs marginally better than chance. The behavioural cues it relies upon are, in 91.3% of cases, scientifically unfounded. This is not a disputed finding. It is the consensus of the largest body of experimental research ever conducted on the topic.

**Second**, this unreliability has a differential impact on traumatised individuals, who constitute the overwhelming majority of the criminal justice population (97% ACE prevalence). Trauma responses are behaviourally indistinguishable from the cues that credibility assessors interpret as deceptive. The system is structurally biased against the population it most frequently processes.

**Third**, evidence-based alternatives exist, are documented, and produce measurable improvements. The New Zealand natural experiment demonstrates that reform produces improvement and reversal produces deterioration — within the same jurisdiction, population, and economic context. Australian pilot programs (Bourke, Koori Courts, MERIT, Drug Courts) demonstrate that effective alternatives are not hypothetical. They exist. They are evaluated. They work. They are not funded or scaled.

The question is not whether the evidence supports reform. It does, unambiguously. The question is whether the institutions responsible for the administration of justice will respond to the evidence or continue to rely on a methodology that the science abandoned decades ago.

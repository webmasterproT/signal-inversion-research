Chloe

Chloe

Liam

Chloe
Yeah, so imagine someone being arrested. They’re stopped suddenly, maybe roughly restrained, their personal belongings taken away — phone, wallet, keys — and then they’re stripped of their clothes, sometimes searched, and placed in a small, bare cell. Often, the walls are painted in dull, muted colors designed to minimize stimulation.
Liam
That’s right. Sociologist Erving Goffman called this process the “mortification of self.” It’s a systematic stripping away of identity and agency. The person goes from being a citizen to an object of institutional control.
Chloe
And it’s not just symbolic. There are real neurobiological effects. The sudden arrest triggers an acute stress response — cortisol and adrenaline flood the system, heart rate spikes, and the prefrontal cortex, which governs rational thinking and impulse control, gets impaired.
Liam
So the person’s ability to think clearly, resist suggestion, or make reasoned decisions is compromised before they even enter the interrogation room.
Chloe
Add to that sleep deprivation, sensory monotony from the bare cell, and social isolation. All these factors compound cognitive impairment and increase suggestibility.
Liam
That’s where Gudjonsson’s model of interrogative suggestibility comes in. It breaks suggestibility into two parts: yield — the tendency to give in to leading questions — and shift — changing answers after negative feedback. Both are elevated by these detention conditions.
Chloe
And here’s the kicker: the legal doctrine of voluntariness assumes the person giving a confession is a rational, autonomous agent. But the detained person is neurobiologically and psychologically compromised. So the idea of a “voluntary” confession becomes, well, empirically incoherent.
Liam
Yet courts often ignore all this. They focus only on what happens in the interview room, not on the hours of detention that precede it.
Chloe
It’s like the person who walks into the interrogation room isn’t the same person who was arrested hours earlier. Their cognitive and emotional state has been fundamentally altered.
Liam
That sets the stage for what happens next — the interrogation itself. The dominant method in many common law countries is the Reid Technique.
Chloe
Yeah, the Reid Technique is a two-stage process. First, there’s the Behavioural Analysis Interview, or BAI, which is supposed to detect deception through behavioral cues. Then comes the nine-step interrogation designed to extract a confession.
Liam
But here’s the problem: trained investigators’ ability to detect deception is barely better than chance — about 54% accuracy. And yet, their confidence in their judgments is high. This phenomenon is called “confidence without accuracy.”
Chloe
And the behaviors they interpret as signs of deception — like avoiding eye contact, hesitating, fidgeting — are actually typical responses of innocent people under stress.
Liam
So an innocent person, scared and confused, might avoid eye contact or hesitate, and the investigator reads that as lying.
Chloe
Let’s walk through some of the nine steps. Step one is positive confrontation — the investigator tells the suspect they’re guilty, presenting it as a fact, regardless of evidence.
Liam
That’s a performative speech act. It’s not just stating a fact; it’s trying to create a reality where the suspect’s guilt is already established.
Chloe
Then step two is theme development — offering a morally minimizing narrative to make confession psychologically easier. Like, “It was a momentary lapse,” or “Anyone might have done the same.”
Liam
This reduces the psychological cost of confessing, even for innocent people. They’re faced with a choice: accept this bearable story or endure continued interrogation in a punishing environment.
Chloe
Steps three to six involve managing denial — interrupting and blocking the suspect’s attempts to tell their own story. So the suspect can’t present a coherent alternative narrative.
Liam
Which is devastating for innocent people, because their denials get truncated and challenged, making them seem less credible.
Chloe
Step seven is the alternative question — a linguistic trap that presupposes guilt by offering a choice between two guilty scenarios. For example, “Did you plan this or was it a crime of passion?”
Liam
Answering either way admits the act. It’s a presuppositional trap that’s hard to escape.
Chloe
Finally, steps eight and nine involve expanding the oral confession into a detailed narrative and then reducing it to a written statement. But here, the investigator’s language contaminates the confession, sometimes adding details the suspect couldn’t have known.
Liam
So the confession is partly the investigator’s narrative, presented as the suspect’s own.
Chloe
The Reid Technique is designed to produce confessions, not necessarily to find the truth.
Liam
And the false confession literature backs this up. Studies show that between 12 and 30 percent of exonerations involve false confessions. Vulnerable groups like juveniles and people with intellectual disabilities are disproportionately affected.
Chloe
There’s also the phenomenon of internalized false confessions, where suspects come to believe they committed the crime because of the interrogation pressure and memory distortion.
Liam
That ties into the next big topic — the science of deception detection and what’s called the Signal Inversion Effect.
Chloe
Right. Meta-analyses show that human deception detection accuracy is about 54 percent — barely better than flipping a coin. And when it comes to detecting lies specifically, accuracy drops to 47 percent, which is actually worse than chance.
Liam
What’s more, the cues people believe indicate lying — like gaze aversion, nervousness, disfluency, and hedging — are mostly wrong or even inverted.
Chloe
The Signal Inversion Effect means that authentic cognitive effort and truthful speech produce behaviors like disfluency, hesitation, and gaze aversion, which observers misinterpret as signs of deception.
Liam
In one study analyzing real trial testimony, truthful speakers produced significantly more disfluencies — those “ums” and “uhs” — than deceptive speakers, with a medium effect size six times larger than typical deception cues.
Chloe
And in linguistic analyses of true versus false confessions, false confessions had much higher rates of impersonal pronouns like “it” and “that,” and second-person pronouns like “you.” This reflects linguistic distancing and orientation toward the interrogator.
Liam
So innocent people coerced into confessing tend to frame the narrative impersonally and refer to the interrogator, because the story isn’t theirs.
Chloe
Meanwhile, confident, fluent delivery is more typical of deception, while hesitant, fragmented speech is more typical of truth.
Liam
The implications are huge. Judges, jurors, and investigators rely on heuristics that systematically misread truthful behavior as deceptive.
Chloe

Liam
There’s also a behavioral adaptation feedback loop. People who are repeatedly disbelieved despite telling the truth may learn to suppress their authentic speech patterns and adopt performed behaviors — steady eye contact, fluent delivery — that match expectations of honesty.
Chloe
Which ironically makes truthful people sound more like liars, complicating detection even further.
Liam
It’s like the folk psychology of deception is a broken compass pointing the wrong way.
Chloe

Liam
Elizabeth Loftus’s research on memory malleability shows that the language used in questioning can alter not just testimony but actual memory.

About one in five witnesses subjected to post-event linguistic manipulation develop materially altered memories.

Cross-examination uses leading questions that embed presuppositions, effectively rewriting witness memories.

So the cross-examiner isn’t just testing memories; they’re editing them.
Chloe
Expert witnesses and prior conviction evidence also contribute to constructing a narrative of guilt that jurors can’t easily disentangle.

And judicial instructions are often misunderstood, so jurors apply commonsense moral intuitions rather than legal standards.
Chloe
The courtroom is less a truth-finding arena and more a narrative contest.
Liam

Media coverage constructs guilt narratives before trial, often using linguistic framing that presupposes guilt.
Liam
Framing theory tells us that how information is presented shapes what questions audiences ask, what causes they infer, and what moral judgments they make.

Social media and viral content have collapsed the temporal sequencing that once separated pre-trial publicity from the trial itself.

Judicial instructions to disregard media exposure are largely ineffective.

Pennington and Hastie’s story model says jurors construct a coherent narrative and then match verdicts to that story.

Cognitive schemas shape how jurors interpret evidence, often unconsciously.

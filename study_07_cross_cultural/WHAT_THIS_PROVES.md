# What This Proves

## What we tested

We analysed how truthful people from different cultures (US, India, Mexico, Romania) speak — specifically their use of hedging ("I think", "maybe"), certainty markers ("definitely", "I know"), and disfluencies ("um", "uh"). The question: when truth-tellers from different cultures talk, do they sound the same?

Then we built a simple lie detector calibrated on American speech patterns and pointed it at truthful speakers from other cultures.

## What we found

Truthful speech is culturally specific. An Indian truth-teller uses hedging differently from an American truth-teller. A Mexican truth-teller uses certainty markers at different rates. These differences are statistically significant — they're not noise, they're real patterns shaped by culture, not deception.

When we ran the American-calibrated detector on truthful speakers from other cultures, it flagged them as deceptive at significantly higher rates. Not because they were lying. Because their truth sounds different.

This is the same signal inversion from Study 3, but with a cultural multiplier. If you're from a culture that hedges more when being honest (many non-Anglo cultures do), an Anglo lie detector reads your honesty as evasion. If your culture uses less direct eye contact as a sign of respect, the system reads it as guilt.

## Why it matters

Every courtroom has a cultural baseline, even if nobody admits it. The jury's mental model of "what an honest person looks like" is built from their own cultural experience. An Aboriginal witness whose truthful speech follows different norms will be read as deceptive — not because of anything they did, but because their truth is linguistically illegible to the instrument.

This isn't about accent or language fluency. It's about deep patterns in how honesty sounds across cultures. The system is monocultural. The population isn't.

## The numbers (simplified)

- **4 cultures** compared (US, India, Mexico, Romania)
- Truthful speech patterns differ significantly across cultures (Kruskal-Wallis test, p < .05 for key features)
- An American-calibrated detector produces **higher false-positive rates** for non-American truth-tellers
- The effect is strongest for hedging and certainty markers — the exact cues courts use to assess credibility

## So what?

If you speak the truth in a way that doesn't sound like white American truth, the system hears a lie. Your culture becomes your criminal record.

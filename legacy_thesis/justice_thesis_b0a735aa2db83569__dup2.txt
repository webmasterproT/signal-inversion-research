 SWER = Single Wire Earth Return. It's actually used right now in rural Australia (and NZ, parts of Africa, Brazil). Instead of running two wires to a remote
   farm (active + neutral), they run ONE wire out and use the ground itself as the return path for the current. It literally does what the copper coil paper
  proposes — uses the Earth as a conductor.

  The catch: it works because in those situations running a second wire to a property 50km away costs more than the electricity you lose through the ground.
  It's a deliberate tradeoff — accept worse efficiency because the alternative (two wires across the outback) is too expensive for a handful of farms drawing
  small amounts of power.

  So it actually proves Earth conduction is real and works. But it also proves the losses are significant enough that you'd only do it when the infrastructure
   cost of proper wiring is even worse. That's the opposite of what the paper argues — the paper says Earth conduction is better than wire. SWER shows it's a
  compromise you tolerate when you have no better option.

  The paper should actually reference SWER data directly. It would be honest evidence — "this works at small scale, here are the measured losses, here's our
  argument for why coil-boosted voltage could reduce those losses." That would be a much stronger paper than the current version which skips over the
  real-world numbers.

# Signal Inversion Effect (Deception Cues and Truthful Speech)

This folder holds the **Signal Inversion Effect** working paper and related preprint materials: how authentic cognitive and linguistic behaviour is often mistaken for deception, with evidence from trial testimony, confessions, and a global belief–evidence comparison.

Across four studies, the work reports that truthful trial testimony shows **higher disfluency** than deceptive testimony (Mann–Whitney **p = .004**, **d = 0.60**), that false confessions diverge sharply from true confessions on pronoun patterns (e.g. **“you” ~7.6×** more in false confessions), that **21 of 23** matched public “deception cues” are inverted relative to meta-analytic evidence (**91.3%**, binomial **p < .0001**; weighted inversion index **0.81**), and that human lie-detection sits near chance (**~54%** overall; **~47%** for lies specifically) while linguistic classifiers reach **~63.5–83%**.

**14 Goals:** Strongest links — **Goal 4** (courts and epistemic legitimacy of verdicts), **Goal 3** (wrongful conviction and confession dynamics), **Goal 5** (police/interrogation practice and demeanour evidence).

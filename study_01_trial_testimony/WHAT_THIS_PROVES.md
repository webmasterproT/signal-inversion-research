# What This Proves

## What we tested

We took 121 clips from real criminal trials — 60 where the person was telling the truth, 61 where they were lying — and trained a computer to spot the difference using linguistic markers (things like how often someone says "um", uses hedging words, or speaks in first person).

## What we found

The single strongest signal was disfluency — the "ums" and "ahs" and stumbling over words. Except it goes the **opposite direction** to what everyone assumes. People who stumble over their words more are **more likely to be telling the truth**, not less. Effect size d = 0.60, which is 6x larger than the typical deception cue.

The best the computer could do with all the linguistic features combined was 63.5% accuracy. Humans watching the same kind of footage? 54%. A coin flip is 50%.

Most of the cues courts and juries rely on — confidence, eye contact, smooth delivery — showed **no statistically significant difference** between liars and truth-tellers. Seven out of eight markers came back "ns" (not significant). The system is reading noise.

## Why it matters

Courts let juries assess "demeanour" as evidence of credibility. Judges instruct them to watch how someone speaks. The one thing that actually does differ between truth and lies — disfluency — goes in the opposite direction to what the instruction assumes. A nervous, stumbling witness is more likely honest. A smooth, confident one might be rehearsed.

## The numbers (simplified)

- **121** real trial clips analysed
- **54%** — human accuracy at spotting lies (barely better than flipping a coin)
- **63.5%** — best a computer can do with linguistic markers
- **d = 0.60** — disfluency effect (truth-tellers stumble more), 6x bigger than the average deception cue
- **7 out of 8** markers showed no significant difference between liars and truth-tellers

## So what?

If you've ever been nervous on a witness stand and worried it made you look guilty — you were right to worry. The system reads your honesty as deception.

10 People Before Nuremberg.1 Person After.A Record of What Was Known, What Was Done,and What That Means for Everyone Inside the SystemThis document is a statement of record.It is archived permanently.It has been provided formally to every oversight bodyin every Australian jurisdiction.From the date of this record, there is no defence of ignorance.
A Note to the ReaderYou do not need to know anything before reading this.If you have heard the word Nuremberg and are not sure what it refers to, here is everything you need to know:After the Second World War, a series of trials were held to hold accountable the people who had operated the Nazi system ﾑ not just the leaders, but the judges, the lawyers, the doctors, the administrators, the people who ran the trains. The trials established something that had not existed as a formal legal principle before: that knowing about a harm and continuing to participate in it is itself an act. That following orders is not a defence. That being part of a system producing documented harm, even from a distance, creates responsibility.That is all you need to know.This document applies that principle to the Australian criminal justice system.It does not require that you are a lawyer. It does not require that you have read the research. The research is summarised inside, in plain language. Every claim is explained from the beginning. If you can read this sentence, you can read everything that follows.The argument is simple:We know the tools the justice system uses to determine guilt do not work. We have known this for decades. The system has continued anyway. Every person inside that system ﾑ who interrogates, prosecutes, judges, manages, trains, administers, or simply does not ask ﾑ is inside this argument.That includes you, if you are reading this as someone who works in the system.It also includes everyone who votes, pays taxes, and has assumed without asking that the process figures it out.The process does not figure it out.This document is the record of what the process actually does.The title requires no explanation beyond this:Before the Nuremberg trials, there were ten kinds of people operating the system. After the trials, there was only one: the person who had to account for what they did with what they knew.That is where we are now.
Part One: Statement of RecordThis is not a hypothesis.We are not raising concerns. We are not calling for further research. We are placing on record what is known, by whom it is known, and when they knew it.What follows is a statement of fact. It is intended to be read twice: once now, and once later, when the question is no longer whether the harm occurred but what we call the people who permitted it to continue.I. What We Know About MemoryIn 1974, Elizabeth Loftus demonstrated that changing a single word in a question caused 32% of subjects to remember broken glass that was never there.Not the facts. Not the allegation. One word.This has been replicated for fifty years. The aggregate false memory rate across her research programme is approximately 22%. One in five witnesses, subjected to the ordinary conditions of interrogation and cross-examination, will carry a fabricated memory into court and deliver it under oath with complete sincerity.They are not lying. They are remembering what the questions built for them.Every interrogation conducted after 1974 occurred after we knew this. Every conviction that rested on witness testimony occurred after we knew this.We continued.II. What We Know About Lie DetectionIn 2006, Bond and DePaulo published the definitive account of human deception detection. Two hundred and six studies. Twenty-four thousand four hundred and eighty-three judges ﾑ police officers, customs officials, trained investigators of every kind.Overall accuracy: 54.1%. Chance is 50%.For lie detection specifically: 47%. Below chance.The more experienced the investigator, the more confident their assessments. The more confident, the more wrong. Training does not improve accuracy. It improves certainty while accuracy stays essentially random.Every court has a person whose job is to assess credibility. Every jury has twelve. Every trial hinges on it. We have known since 2006, with a sample of twenty-four thousand, that this capacity does not exist at above-chance levels in any trained human population.We continued.III. What We Know About What People Think They KnowIn 2006, the Global Deception Research Team surveyed eleven thousand people across seventy-five countries. They asked: what does a liar look like?Sixty-four percent of people worldwide believe liars avoid eye contact. The actual effect size of gaze aversion as a deception indicator: d = 0.05. Functionally zero.When we matched the full list of commonly held beliefs against the actual empirical data on deception cues, ninety-one point three percent of the cues people use to assess guilt are either unrelated to deception or move in the opposite direction.The framework is not merely inaccurate. It is inverted. The system has been using an inverted compass for the entirety of its existence, and the people using it have grown more confident the further wrong they have traveled.Every juror who has ever assessed credibility has done so using a belief system that is, by the weight of the available evidence, backwards.We continued.IV. What We Know About ConfessionsWhen DNA testing became available it created something that had not existed before: certainty.We began going back. Between 12% and 30% of people exonerated by DNA evidence had confessed. Detailed, signed confessions. Confessions containing specific information that investigators told juries only a guilty person could know ﾑ because investigators had provided that information during the interrogation.Here is what must be understood: a confession case is not a separate category from a heuristics case. It is not outside this indictment. The confession was produced inside the same room, by the same methods, under the same conditions ﾑ conditions we know elevate a person's suggestibility between 80% and 120% above their normal baseline before the first question is asked. The confession is not independent evidence. It is the output of the broken process.We continued.V. What Incarceration IsRemove the justification narrative. Remove the word "criminal." Remove "convicted." Remove "sentenced."What remains is this: a human being, in a cage, placed there by a state, against their will, unable to leave.This is the structure of incarceration. This is also the structure of a concentration camp.The difference is not structural. The difference is the story told to authorise it.We know the process produces false outcomes at a documented and non-trivial rate. We know the tools it uses are inverted. We know the confessions it extracts are unreliable. We know the witnesses it relies on carry memories shaped by the questions.Which means: inside those cages, right now, are people placed there by a process we know is broken.We continued anyway.VI. The Question That RemainsThe question is not whether harm has occurred. The question is not whether the evidence was available to those with the power to act.The question ﾑ the only question that will matter when we look back at this ﾑ is what we call people who had authority, had evidence, and chose continuation.We have answered that question before.We are placing this on record so that when the time comes to answer it again, there is no defence of ignorance available.The record exists. It exists now.
Part Two: I Didn't KnowI didn't know.That is what I would like to say. That is what I am preparing to say. I have been preparing it for some time now, and I think I have it right. I didn't know. How could I have known. I was just doing my job. I was just living my life.I need you to understand that I am not a bad person.I am a juror.I sat in a room and I listened and I watched the person across the room and I made a judgment about whether they were telling the truth. I used what I knew. What everyone knows. The way they looked away when they answered. The way their voice changed.I didn't know that sixty-four percent of people worldwide share my belief about eye contact and that it has no relationship to whether someone is lying. No one told me. No one tells jurors that. They put me in a room and told me to use my judgment.I used my judgment. I went home. I had dinner. I did not think about it much after that.I am a detective.I have twenty-two years on the job. I have interviewed thousands of people. I know, in my body, what a liar looks like.I am told now that the research says I operate at 54% accuracy. Barely above a coin flip. I don't believe it. I can't believe it, because if I believe it then I have to think about the twenty-two years. I have to think about all of them.So I don't believe it. I retire next year. I have a good pension. I didn't know.I am a prosecutor.I had a confession. Signed. Detailed. I have read since then about how interrogations work. About how those details get transferred. About how a person who has been awake for thirty hours, sitting in a custody suite, under conditions we now know elevate suggestibility by 80 to 120 percent ﾑ about how that person comes to know the colour of the carpet.I had a confession. I believed in the confession. I presented it.The person is still inside. I didn't know. Not fully. Not in a way I could have acted on. That's what I tell myself.I am a judge.I am aware of the research. I have read summaries of it. I have concerns about its implications, which I have not acted on because acting on them would require a unilateral departure from established evidentiary frameworks, which is not my role.I didn't know what to do with what I knew. Does that count.I am a politician.I ran on it. Tough on crime. Safe streets. Lock them up. I said those things and people cheered and I won. I am not stupid. I know the research exists.I also know what happens to politicians who say that. I didn't know what to do with that either. I have a family. I have a career. I am not a monster. I am a person inside a system that rewards certain choices and punishes others and I made the choices that were rewarded.That is, I understand now, exactly the same thing everyone else is saying.I am a store clerk.I voted. I complained about crime when it was on the news. I didn't think much about the people inside the cages because I was busy.I didn't know that the person who assessed credibility was guessing. I didn't know the confession might have been extracted. I didn't know that the framework everyone uses to decide if someone is lying is ninety-one percent wrong.But. I also didn't ask.I am writing this now because I understand something about what is coming.The proceedings will not primarily be about what people did not know. They will be about what people knew and what they did with it.I understand that "I didn't know" and "I didn't ask" are not the same thing. I understand that participating in a system producing a known and documented harm ﾑ even from the furthest edge, even as a voter, even as a reader of newspapers ﾑ is still participation.I am not writing this to absolve myself.I am writing this because the alternative is waiting for someone else to write it about me.
Part Three: I Believed In ItI believed in it.Before all of this. Before the knock on the door and the handcuffs and the room and the questions that went on so long I stopped being able to tell which thoughts were mine. Before I signed something I don't fully remember signing.I believed in it. I voted for the people who said they would make it stronger.I am on bail right now.I did not do what they say I did. I know that sounds like what everyone says. I know that now in a way I did not know it before.The process looked at the way I spoke in that room ﾑ the way I hesitated, the way I couldn't look at them, the way I kept saying I don't know, I'm not sure, I can't remember exactly ﾑ and the process decided that meant something. I know now what it actually meant. It meant I was trying to tell the truth.The process saw guilt. I was showing them what honesty looks like, and they were reading it as guilt, and I didn't know that, and they didn't know that, and possibly no one in that room knew that ﾑ but the research knew it. The research has known it for fifty years. And the room kept going anyway.There is a version of me that is very angry. That version wants to say: the system is corrupt, the system is broken, the system is designed to produce convictions and it produces them regardless of what happened.That version is right.There is another version of me that is harder to be. That version remembers sitting in a jury room. Remembers looking at a person across a courtroom and reading something in the way they held themselves and deciding, in the way you decide things you don't examine, that they seemed guilty.I don't know how to hold both of those things. I am trying to hold both of them because I think not holding both of them is how this keeps going.I nearly signed a confession at four in the morning. Because I thought if I just gave them something they would let me sleep. I thought maybe I was wrong about what I remembered. I thought maybe the exhaustion was the truth and the truth was the exhaustion.I think about the people who did sign. I think about them a lot.I believed in the system that is destroying me.I participated in the conditions that made it possible.I didn't ask questions I could have asked.I am writing this down while I still can.
Part Four: Findings of the TribunalThe Tribunal has heard the evidence. The question before this Tribunal is not what the evidence says. The question is what was done with it, by whom, and whether that constitutes a harm for which responsibility can be assigned.The Tribunal finds that it does.Established FactsOne. Human deception detection operates at a mean accuracy rate of 54.1%, against a chance baseline of 50%. For lie detection specifically the rate is 47% ﾑ below chance. This was established by Bond and DePaulo in 2006 across 206 studies and 24,483 judges, and has not been meaningfully contradicted since.Two. 91.3% of the behavioural cues used by investigators, jurors, and the general public to assess credibility are either empirically unrelated to deception or directionally inverted. Binomial test result: p < 0.0001.Three. Post-event questioning alters the memories of approximately 22% of subjects exposed to it.Four. Pre-interrogation detention conditions produce a compound elevation in suggestibility of 80 to 120% above baseline. Persons in this state cannot be said, in any scientifically defensible sense, to be providing statements voluntarily.Five. Between 12% and 30% of persons exonerated by DNA evidence had provided confessions to crimes they did not commit. This figure is a floor, not a ceiling.Six. Disfluency ﾑ hesitation and stumbling in speech ﾑ is significantly higher in truthful testimony than deceptive testimony. This is the opposite of what courts have been trained to believe.On KnowledgeThe defence submitted that those responsible did not know.The Tribunal does not accept this submission.The evidence was published. It was peer-reviewed. It was available in the libraries of every law school, medical school, and police training facility in every jurisdiction. The Tribunal draws a distinction between not knowing and not asking. The institution knew. The institution continued.On IntentThe Tribunal finds no evidence of a conspiracy to convict the innocent. The Tribunal accepts that most participants believed they were doing their jobs correctly.The Tribunal notes that this is also what was submitted at the previous proceedings from which this Tribunal draws its authority and its precedent.Intent is not the standard. The standard is knowledge, capacity, and continuation.Applying the System to ItselfThe Tribunal now applies, in full, the same system. The same evidentiary standards. The same credibility assessment framework. The same interrogation conditions. The same presumptions.The accused will be assessed by the tools they built, maintained, and defended.The Tribunal observes the accused. Several displayed avoidance behaviours when questioned. Several said they could not remember. The Tribunal notes that this system, the one they administered, treated "I can't remember" as evasion.The Tribunal finds the accused credible in the same proportion that trained investigators find anyone credible.Which is to say: 54.1%. Which is to say: the Tribunal is, by the standard of the system being applied, essentially guessing.The accused built a system that guesses and calls it justice. The accused will be judged by a system that guesses and calls it justice.The Tribunal finds all accused guilty on all counts.

They will be placed in a room. It is not accepted here.Verdict: Guilty ﾑ least culpable of those before the Tribunal.Sentence: Community service in direct contact with the people they administered. They will learn their names. Part Seven: What Was Done InsideThe Tribunal has documented the mechanism. The Tribunal has named the actors. The Tribunal now records what the mechanism produces when it reaches a human being.Not a category of human being. A human being.A person was placed in a cell.A psychologist came.The psychologist observed that the person could form words on the phone with their friend but could not form words in the room.The psychologist noted this. And then the psychologist said: therefore you are faking.The Tribunal pauses here.A trained clinical professional observed a presentation consistent with Functional Neurological Disorder ﾑ a condition in which neurological symptoms are real, documented, and specifically known to fluctuate with stress, context, and relationship safety. A person may speak to a trusted person on the phone and lose speech in a room with a stranger who holds authority over them. This is not a paradox. This is the condition.The psychologist saw the symptom. The psychologist described the symptom accurately. The psychologist then used their accurate description of the symptom as evidence that the symptom was not real.This is not ignorance. This is not a mistake born of insufficient information. This is a professional, with access to clinical literature, using professional authority to confirm a predetermined conclusion using the person's own symptoms as proof.The Tribunal finds that this required knowledge to execute. You cannot use a symptom as evidence of its own fabrication without first knowing enough about the symptom to describe it. The psychologist knew.The person was stripped.The Tribunal records this without elaboration because elaboration is not required. A human being, in the custody of the state, was stripped naked. This constitutes degrading treatment under any definition the system has ever applied to anyone else.The person vomited in the cell. The person asked for cleaning products. None were provided.The Tribunal finds that a person in a cell who has vomited and asked for cleaning products and been refused has been refused by a person who made a decision. That person made a decision. That decision has a name.A person in authority used a word. The Tribunal will not repeat the word. The Tribunal notes that the word was chosen. A person with professional authority, in a position of complete power over another person, chose a word that communicated exactly what the system thought of them.FindingThe Tribunal finds that what was done to this person inside the cage was not incidental to the system. It was not the behaviour of bad actors who deviated from an otherwise sound process.It was the system operating on a person the system had categorised, using the inverted framework, as manipulative, as not credible, as not deserving of the protections the system nominally provides.The degradation. The isolation. The vomit on the floor. The word. All of it authorised by a broken process. All of it done to a human being.

Part Ten: The Trap ClosesThe judgment has been delivered. The sentences have been handed down. The Tribunal now turns to itself.The Nuremberg judges used the same tools. They assessed credibility. They weighed confession evidence. They applied a framework for determining truth that the research has since found to be operating at chance level. They were human beings making judgments about other human beings using the same inverted compass that has been used in every courtroom since courts existed.Applied completely ﾑ applied without exception, applied to everyone including the people holding the gavel ﾑ no one gets out.This is why the current system cannot try itself. Not because it lacks the will. Because the people who would conduct the trial are inside the indictment. Everyone is in the machine. Everyone has been making the machine run.Punishment Is Not JusticePunishing the interrogator who extracted the false confession does not give back the years of the person who served the sentence. Imprisoning the judge does not rebuild what was taken.Punishment is always too late. It arrives after the harm. It does not travel back and stop the knock on the door, the room, the questions, the signature, the gate.The only framework that produces justice rather than response to injustice is the one that makes the harm impossible in the first place.Punishment says: this happened, now what. Prevention says: this will not happen. One of those is justice. One of them is the ceremony we perform over the bodies of people the system has already consumed.Circular TimeThe harm that never happens has no file. No case number. No sentencing remarks. No victim statement. No photograph. It is only the absence of a harm that would otherwise have been there.That absence is what justice looks like.Not the gavel. Not the sentence. Not the cage. The absence.Nuremberg was necessary. It was not sufficient. It was the ceremony performed over the bodies.The work in this document is the other thing. The thing that, if it had existed before, would have made Nuremberg unnecessary. The thing that, if it exists now, makes the next Nuremberg unnecessary.The people who wanted blood at Nuremberg were not wrong to want accountability. They were wrong to think that blood was accountability. Accountability is the system that does not produce the harm in the first place.The rest is grief dressed as justice.Justice never had to be here.

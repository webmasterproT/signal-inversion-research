# Cultural Variation in Truthful Speech: The System Uses Culturally Contaminated Cues and Ignores Culturally Stable Ones

OMXUS Research Initiative | research@omxus.com | February 2026 | Preprint -- Not peer reviewed

---

## Abstract

This study examines whether the linguistic features used to assess credibility in criminal justice settings vary across cultures, using the Cross-Cultural Deception Corpus (2014) spanning four cultural groups (US English, Indian English, Romanian, Mexican Spanish). Kruskal-Wallis H-tests with Dunn pairwise comparisons reveal that the features investigators and jurors rely on to assess credibility -- hedging rate (H = 95.83, p < .001), certainty markers (H = 46.39, p < .001), and hedge-to-certainty ratio (H = 90.60, p < .001) -- vary massively across cultures. The largest pairwise difference (Indian vs. Romanian hedging, d = 3.50) exceeds three standard deviations. By contrast, the single most diagnostically powerful marker of truthful speech -- disfluency rate (d = 0.60 in the Perez-Rosas corpus) -- does not vary significantly across cultures (H = 0.91, p = 0.823). First-person pronoun rate (H = 4.70, p = 0.195) and word count (H = 5.69, p = 0.128) are similarly culturally stable. The system relies on the culturally contaminated features and ignores the culturally robust ones. Any credibility assessment instrument calibrated on a single cultural baseline will structurally misread speakers from other cultures.

**Keywords:** cross-cultural, deception detection, credibility assessment, cultural bias, linguistic variation, criminal justice

---

## 1. Introduction

The criminal justice system treats credibility assessment as if the behavioural markers of honesty and deception are universal. The Reid Technique does not have a cultural variation module. Jury instructions on assessing witness credibility do not include a disclaimer that hedging norms differ by three standard deviations between Indian and Romanian speakers. The assumption is that a truthful person looks and sounds the same regardless of cultural background.

This assumption is testable. If the linguistic features used to assess credibility vary significantly across cultures, then every credibility judgment made on a speaker from a non-dominant cultural group is potentially contaminated by cultural distance rather than actual deception.

Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan (2010) demonstrated that psychology's evidence base is overwhelmingly derived from Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, and Democratic (WEIRD) samples. DePaulo et al. (2003), the canonical meta-analysis of deception cues, draws predominantly from Western English-speaking samples. Taylor et al. (2017) showed that linguistic markers of deception shift as a function of cultural background. Bond and Atoum (2000) demonstrated that cross-cultural deception detection accuracy differs from within-culture accuracy.

The question is not whether cultural variation exists. It is whether the specific features the justice system uses to assess credibility are the ones that vary -- and whether the features that are actually diagnostic are the ones that are culturally stable.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Dataset

The Cross-Cultural Deception Corpus (2014) contains spoken narratives from four cultural groups:
- **US English** speakers
- **Indian English** speakers
- **Romanian** speakers
- **Mexican Spanish** speakers

Each speaker provided both truthful and deceptive narratives. This analysis focuses on truthful speech only, testing whether truthful speakers from different cultures produce different linguistic profiles when telling the truth.

### 2.2 Variables

Six linguistic features were extracted:

1. **Hedging Rate** -- epistemic hedges per narrative ("I think," "maybe," "perhaps")
2. **Certainty Rate** -- certainty markers per narrative ("definitely," "absolutely," "clearly")
3. **Hedge:Certainty Ratio** -- relative balance of hedging versus certainty
4. **Disfluency Rate** -- filled pauses, false starts, self-corrections
5. **First-Person Pronoun Rate** -- self-reference frequency
6. **Word Count** -- total words per narrative

### 2.3 Statistical Analysis

Kruskal-Wallis H-tests (non-parametric, appropriate for non-normal distributions) were used to test for cultural variation across the four groups. Where significant, Dunn pairwise comparisons with Bonferroni correction identified which culture pairs differed. Effect sizes calculated as Cohen's d.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Features That Vary Across Cultures

| Feature | H | p | Significant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hedging Rate | 95.83 | < .001 | Yes |
| Certainty Rate | 46.39 | < .001 | Yes |
| Hedge:Certainty Ratio | 90.60 | < .001 | Yes |

These are the features investigators and jurors use to assess credibility. They vary massively.

**Hedging pairwise comparisons (selected):**

| Pair | d | p |
|---|---|---|
| India vs. Romania | 3.50 | < .001 |
| India vs. Mexico | 2.13 | < .001 |
| US vs. India | -1.78 | < .001 |
| US vs. Romania | 1.69 | < .001 |

The India-Romania hedging difference (d = 3.50) means that a truthful Indian speaker and a truthful Romanian speaker, both telling the truth, produce hedging profiles separated by more than three standard deviations. Any credibility heuristic calibrated on one of these cultural baselines will misread the other as either evasive or overconfident.

### 3.2 Features That Do NOT Vary Across Cultures

| Feature | H | p | Significant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Disfluency Rate | 0.91 | 0.823 | No |
| First-Person Pronoun Rate | 4.70 | 0.195 | No |
| Word Count | 5.69 | 0.128 | No |

Disfluency -- the single most diagnostically powerful marker of truth (d = 0.60 in the Perez-Rosas corpus; see companion study) -- does not vary across cultures. No pairwise comparison approaches significance (all p = 1.000).

### 3.3 The Split

The features that carry real diagnostic information (disfluency, d = 0.60) are culturally stable. The features the system actually uses to assess credibility (hedging, certainty, epistemic style) are the most culturally variable. The system is looking at the wrong things, and the wrong things are culturally contaminated.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Implications for Credibility Assessment

An Aboriginal Australian witness whose truthful speech follows different hedging norms than the Anglo-Australian baseline will be read as evasive. A Romanian witness whose certainty patterns differ from the US norm will be read as overconfident. An Indian witness whose hedging rate is 3.5 standard deviations above the Romanian baseline will be read as uncertain or deceptive.

These are not errors in application. They are structural features of any assessment instrument calibrated on a single cultural baseline. The tool cannot distinguish cultural distance from deception because it relies on features that are confounded with culture.

### 4.2 What The System Ignores

Disfluency rate does not vary across cultures (H = 0.91, p = 0.823). If the system assessed credibility using disfluency instead of hedging, it would not systematically misread speakers from non-dominant cultural groups. The culturally robust signal exists. The system ignores it.

First-person pronoun rate is also culturally stable (H = 4.70, p = 0.195). The system ignores this too.

### 4.3 Neurodivergence as a Parallel Case

No public dataset with neurodivergence flags exists, but published experimental studies provide a parallel. Lim, Young, and Brewer (2021; N = 1,410 observers) found that autistic speakers were rated as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical speakers -- when telling the truth. The diagnostic criteria for autism (gaze aversion, flat affect, repetitive movement) overlap near-perfectly with the behavioural cues used to identify deception (Global Deception Research Team, 2006). An autistic person cannot present truthful testimony without involuntarily performing the behaviours the system reads as deception.

Cultural background and neurodivergence produce the same structural outcome: innocence that is illegible to the assessment instrument.

### 4.4 What This Does NOT Mean

This study does not prove that no universal deception cue exists. It shows that the cues the system uses are not universal, and that the cue with the best evidence (disfluency) happens to be universal but is ignored.

---

## 5. Limitations

1. **Four cultures only.** Aboriginal Australian, East Asian, and African cultures are absent from the dataset. These are arguably the most critical groups for the justice-system argument.
2. **Within-culture variation not controlled.** Class, education, and regional dialect effects are not separated from cultural effects.
3. **Neurodivergence argument is literature-based.** The autism-credibility finding comes from published experimental studies, not from analysis of this corpus.
4. **Small samples per culture.** Larger datasets would enable multivariate analysis and interaction effects.

# What This Proves

## What we tested

This one's different — it's not a single experiment. It's the mathematical framework that ties all the other studies together. It asks: how do groups of people (or computers) reach agreement on what's true? And it examines three ways this happens.

## What we found

Three systems that produce consensus (agreement) all work the same way underneath — through **costly signalling** (you have to put something real on the line to be taken seriously):

**1. Bitcoin (computational consensus):** Miners spend real electricity to validate transactions. Cheating costs more than honesty. The maths proves it — the probability of successfully faking a transaction drops exponentially with each confirmation. After 6 confirmations with a 10% attacker, the chance of fraud is 0.03%.

**2. Knowledge distillation (behavioural consensus):** This comes from AI research — a student model learns by watching a teacher model's behaviour, not by being given explicit rules. The parallel to human learning is direct: kids learn behaviour by watching the people around them, not from being lectured. Your "dark knowledge" (the implicit stuff — tone, body language, what's normal) comes from your environment. Behaviour is distilled from surroundings, not rationally chosen.

**3. Web-of-trust (social consensus):** People vouch for each other. Each vouch costs reputation — if the person you vouched for turns out to be dodgy, it reflects on you. This creates a natural Sybil resistance (making it expensive to create fake identities). The cost of attacking the network scales linearly — you can't cheat cheaply.

## Why it matters

The criminal justice system claims to produce consensus about truth (guilty/not guilty). But Studies 1-9 show it fails on every empirical measure. This framework shows what actual consensus mechanisms look like — and none of them resemble a courtroom. Real consensus requires costly signalling, empirical validation, and self-correction. Courts require authority, ritual, and finality.

The distillation piece is especially important: if behaviour is learned from environment (like language — Study 8), then punishing individuals for environmentally-produced behaviour is like punishing a student model for learning what its teacher taught.

## The numbers (simplified)

- **Bitcoin double-spend** probability at 6 confirmations, 10% attacker: **0.03%**
- Probability drops **exponentially** with each confirmation
- Vouch-based Sybil attack cost scales **linearly** — no cheap way to game it
- Framework validated against Nakamoto (2008), Hinton (2015), Bandura (1977)

## So what?

We have mathematical proof of what real truth-finding looks like. It looks nothing like a courtroom. Courts produce verdicts. Consensus mechanisms produce truth. They're not the same thing, and now there's a framework that shows exactly where they diverge.

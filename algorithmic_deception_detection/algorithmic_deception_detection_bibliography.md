## Background Literature

**Bond, C. F., & DePaulo, B. M. (2008)**. Individual differences in judging deception: Accuracy and bias. *Psychological Bulletin*, 134(4), 477–492. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.134.4.477
> Topical relevance: Extends Bond & DePaulo (2006) by examining individual variability in human deception detection, providing context for why algorithmic approaches may outperform even the best human judges.

**DePaulo, B. M., Lindsay, J. J., Malone, B. E., Muhlenbruck, L., Charlton, K., & Cooper, H. (2003)**. Cues to deception. *Psychological Bulletin*, 129(1), 74–118. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.129.1.74
> Topical relevance: The foundational meta-analysis cataloguing behavioural and verbal cues to deception, establishing which features machine learning classifiers might exploit and which cues are unreliable.

**Vrij, A. (2008)**. *Detecting lies and deceit: Pitfalls and opportunities* (2nd ed.). Wiley.
> Topical relevance: Comprehensive textbook synthesising decades of deception research, covering the theoretical limitations of human detection that motivate algorithmic alternatives.

**Vrij, A., Hartwig, M., & Granhag, P. A. (2019)**. Reading lies: Nonverbal communication and deception. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 70, 295–317. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-psych-010418-103135
> Topical relevance: Reviews the state of nonverbal deception cue research and its poor reliability, underscoring why computational linguistic approaches may offer more consistent detection performance.

**Mihalcea, R., & Strapparava, C. (2009)**. The lie detector: Explorations in the automatic recognition of deceptive language. *Proceedings of the ACL-IJCNLP 2009 Conference Short Papers*, 309–312.
> Topical relevance: An early NLP-based deception detection study using text classification, establishing precedent for the machine learning pipeline applied in this study.

**Ott, M., Choi, Y., Cardie, C., & Hancock, J. T. (2011)**. Finding deceptive opinion spam by any stretch of the imagination. *Proceedings of the 49th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics*, 309–319.
> Topical relevance: Demonstrates that linguistic features alone can distinguish fabricated from genuine text at scale, a key premise underlying automated deception detection in trial testimony.

**Pérez-Rosas, V., & Mihalcea, R. (2015)**. Experiments in open domain deception detection. *Proceedings of the 2015 Conference on Empirical Methods in Natural Language Processing*, 1120–1125.
> Topical relevance: Extends deception detection beyond constrained domains, testing whether classifiers generalise across deception contexts — directly relevant to the ecological validity of trial-based models.

**Kassin, S. M., Drizin, S. A., Grisso, T., Gudjonsson, G. H., Leo, R. A., & Redlich, A. D. (2010)**. Police-induced confessions: Risk factors and recommendations. *Law and Human Behavior*, 34(1), 3–38. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10979-009-9188-6
> Topical relevance: Documents how coercive interrogation produces false confessions that algorithmic classifiers trained on courtroom data must contend with, raising questions about ground-truth labelling validity.

**National Research Council (2009)**. *Strengthening forensic science in the United States: A path forward*. National Academies Press.
> Topical relevance: Landmark report identifying systemic weaknesses in forensic evidence reliability, providing the institutional context for why automated, evidence-based deception tools are being pursued.

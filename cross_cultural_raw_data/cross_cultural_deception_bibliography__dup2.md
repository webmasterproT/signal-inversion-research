## Background Literature

**The Global Deception Research Team (2006)**. A world of lies. *Journal of Cross-Cultural Psychology*, 37(1), 60–74. https://doi.org/10.1177/0022022105282295
> Topical relevance: The largest cross-national survey of deception beliefs and behaviours, establishing that lying frequency and perceived acceptability vary significantly across cultures — the central premise of cross-cultural deception research.

**Bond, C. F., & Atoum, A. O. (2000)**. International deception. *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 26(3), 385–395. https://doi.org/10.1177/0146167200265010
> Topical relevance: One of the first studies demonstrating that cross-cultural deception detection accuracy differs from within-culture accuracy, raising the question of whether deception cues are culturally universal or specific.

**Taylor, P. J., Larner, S., Conchie, S. M., & Menacere, T. (2017)**. Culture moderates changes in linguistic self-presentation and detail provision when deceiving others. *Royal Society Open Science*, 4(6), 170128. https://doi.org/10.1098/rsos.170128
> Topical relevance: Directly demonstrates that the linguistic markers of deception (detail quantity, self-references) shift as a function of cultural background, supporting the hypothesis that detection tools calibrated on one culture may misfire on another.

**Leal, S., Vrij, A., Mann, S., & Fisher, R. P. (2015)**. Who is lying, the narrator or the translator? Adjusting credibility assessments for cross-cultural communication. *Psychiatry, Psychology and Law*, 22(3), 350–358.
> Topical relevance: Examines how interpreter-mediated interviews compound cross-cultural credibility assessment errors, relevant to understanding confounds in multilingual deception datasets.

**Vrij, A., & Semin, G. R. (1996)**. Lie experts' beliefs about nonverbal indicators of deception. *Journal of Nonverbal Behavior*, 20(1), 65–80. https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02248715
> Topical relevance: Documents how professional lie detectors hold culturally shaped beliefs about deception cues, showing that even expert judgments carry cultural bias.

**Bogaard, G., Meijer, E. H., Vrij, A., & Merckelbach, H. (2016)**. Strong, but wrong: Lay people's and police officers' beliefs about verbal and nonverbal cues to deception. *PLOS ONE*, 11(6), e0156615. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0156615
> Topical relevance: Shows that both lay people and police endorse deception cues unsupported by evidence, highlighting how culturally entrenched stereotypes about lying behaviour persist across professional training.

**DePaulo, B. M., Lindsay, J. J., Malone, B. E., Muhlenbruck, L., Charlton, K., & Cooper, H. (2003)**. Cues to deception. *Psychological Bulletin*, 129(1), 74–118. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.129.1.74
> Topical relevance: The canonical meta-analysis of deception cues, whose predominantly Western samples raise the question of whether findings generalise across the cultural groups examined in this study.

**Vrij, A. (2008)**. *Detecting lies and deceit: Pitfalls and opportunities* (2nd ed.). Wiley.
> Topical relevance: Comprehensive reference on deception detection methodology, providing the theoretical framework against which cross-cultural variation in cue reliability can be evaluated.

**Levine, T. R. (2014)**. Truth-default theory (TDT): A theory of human deception and deception detection. *Journal of Language and Social Psychology*, 33(4), 378–392. https://doi.org/10.1177/0261927X14535916
> Topical relevance: Proposes that humans default to believing others are truthful, a cognitive tendency that may interact with cultural norms around politeness and face-saving to produce different detection thresholds across cultures.

**Matsumoto, D., & Hwang, H. S. (2011)**. Judging faces in context. *Social and Personality Psychology Compass*, 5(5), 264–277. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1751-9004.2011.00348.x
> Topical relevance: Demonstrates that cultural context modulates facial expression interpretation, directly relevant to whether nonverbal deception cues are read differently across cultural groups.

**Henrich, J., Heine, S. J., & Norenzayan, A. (2010)**. The weirdest people in the world? *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 33(2-3), 61–83. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X0999152X
> Topical relevance: The influential critique of psychology's over-reliance on Western, educated, industrialised samples, providing the methodological rationale for examining deception phenomena cross-culturally.

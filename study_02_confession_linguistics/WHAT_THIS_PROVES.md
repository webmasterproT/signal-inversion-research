# What This Proves

## What we tested

We analysed the actual words used in 135 confessions — 98 that were presumed true and 37 that were later **proven false** (the person didn't do it). We counted specific words and compared how often they appeared in each group.

## What we found

False confessions are linguistically different from true ones, but not in the way anyone expected. People who falsely confessed used dramatically more words like:

- **"you"** — 7.59x more often (as in "you said", "you told me")
- **"that's"** — 11.67x more often (as in "that's what happened")
- **"it's"** — 12.17x more often (as in "it's like you said")

These are not signs of someone making up a story. They're signs of someone **repeating back what they were told**. The false confessor is echoing the interrogator's script. "You said I did it, so that's what happened."

True confessors use first-person language — "I did", "I went", "I felt." They own the narrative because it's theirs. False confessors use impersonal and second-person language because the narrative was **fed to them**.

## Why it matters

Courts treat a confession as the strongest possible evidence. Once someone confesses, juries almost always convict. But the linguistic fingerprint of a false confession is the opposite of what experts assume. A "detailed" confession full of deferential language ("that's right", "it's like you said") doesn't mean someone is owning their guilt. It means someone is complying under pressure.

## The numbers (simplified)

- **135** confessions analysed (37 proven false, 98 presumed true)
- **7.59x** — false confessors said "you" nearly 8 times more often
- **11.67x** — "that's" appeared almost 12 times more in false confessions
- **12.17x** — "it's" appeared over 12 times more in false confessions
- All differences statistically significant (p < .001 — meaning this is not a fluke)

## So what?

When someone in an interrogation room keeps saying "you said" and "that's what happened", they're not confessing. They're parroting. And the system counts it as proof.

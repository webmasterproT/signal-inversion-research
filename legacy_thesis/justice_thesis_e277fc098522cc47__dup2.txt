 The payload idea is the most radical one on the list because it means the app spreads like language — person to person,
  no infrastructure, no permission from any authority. That's conclusion #14 in action. Environment determines everything.
   If the app is in the environment, it propagates.

  The consent part is everything though. The difference between this and malware is exactly one thing: the person asked
  for it. So the flow has to be:

  1. Person A and Person B are physically near each other (BLE range = ~10m)
  2. B sees A's device advertising "VexWallet available" — just a name, nothing transfers
  3. B actively taps/accepts — "I want this"
  4. The HTML payload transfers (~50KB = 2-3 min over BLE)
  5. B opens it. Creates identity. Is now infrastructure too.
  6. B can now give it to Person C.

  That's not an app spreading. That's a ceremony. One human saying to another: here, this is yours now. Like handing
  someone a book. The BLE is just the hand.

  The beautiful part: it's the vouching mechanism AND the distribution mechanism in one gesture. You can't receive the app
   without being near someone who has it. Physical proximity IS the trust layer. No server ever verified anything. A human
   did, by being there.

  The creepy version (auto-broadcast, no consent, installs silently) would destroy the whole thing. The consensual version
   is arguably the most ethical distribution method ever built — it literally cannot spread without a human choosing to
  give it to another human.

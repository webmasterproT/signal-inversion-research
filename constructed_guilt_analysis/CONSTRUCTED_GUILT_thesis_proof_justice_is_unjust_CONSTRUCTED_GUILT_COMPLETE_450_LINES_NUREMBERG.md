# Chapter 1: Introduction

## The Problem of Innocent Behaviour

On any given day in any jurisdiction operating under the common law tradition, a person may be arrested, stripped of their clothing and personal possessions, subjected to an invasive physical search by strangers, confined in a small and deliberately austere cell, and subsequently placed in a closed room from which they are not free to leave — all prior to any finding of guilt, and under the formal constitutional protection of presumptive innocence.

This thesis argues that every element of this sequence is linguistically and institutionally constructed — and that the construction is not incidental but essential.

The argument begins with a deceptively simple proposition: **language does not reflect reality. It constructs it.**

When a person is designated 'guilty,' 'suspicious,' 'a flight risk,' or 'of prior bad character,' these are not descriptive statements. They are speech acts that perform classifications, assign subject positions, and activate institutional machinery.

A second proposition follows necessarily: **if language constructs guilt, then guilt is, in principle, constructable from any body.** The empirical literature on false confessions makes this case with unusual clarity. Kassin and Gudjonsson (2004) documented that between 14% and 25% of exonerated individuals had falsely confessed — a finding that is not anomalous but is rather the predictable output of an interrogation methodology designed to produce confessions rather than truth.

A third proposition is institutional and structural: **the principle of presumptive innocence, while real as legal doctrine, is practically negated at every procedural stage at which it should operate.**

## 2.8 The Signal Inversion Effect: Empirical Foundation

### The Core Finding

**The people who sound most guilty are most likely to be telling the truth.**

This section documents what this thesis terms the **Signal Inversion Effect**: the systematic pattern whereby authentic cognitive and linguistic behaviours are misidentified as indicators of deception, while performed or rehearsed behaviours are mistakenly interpreted as signals of honesty.

### The Inversion Pattern

| Behaviour | What It Actually Signals | What Observers Believe |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Looking away while thinking | Cognitive effort; accessing memory | Shifty; hiding something |
| Saying "I think" or "maybe" | Genuine uncertainty; honest retrieval | Evasive; lying |
| Fragmented narrative | How real memory works (especially trauma) | Fabricating; inconsistent |
| Saying "I don't know" | Accurate self-knowledge | Uncooperative; guilty |
| Confident, fluent delivery | Rehearsed narrative; possible deception | Honest; credible |

### Empirical Evidence: Deception Detection Accuracy

**Table 2.8.1: Meta-Analytic Summary (Bond & DePaulo, 2006)**

| Study | N (Judges) | Accuracy % | 95% CI |
|-------|------------|------------|--------|
| Weighted mean (247 studies) | 24,483 | **54.1%** | [53.7, 54.9] |
| Chance baseline | — | 50.0% | — |
| **Advantage over chance** | — | **4.1 percentage points** | — |

**Interpretation:** Trained investigators perform barely above chance. A system that generates false accusation at a rate of approximately 23% among those subjected to it cannot be described as a truth-finding mechanism.

### Memory Distortion: Language as Memory Author

**Table 2.8.2: Memory Distortion Effect Sizes (Loftus, 2005)**

| Study | Effect Size (d) | False Memory % |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| Loftus & Palmer (1974) — verb change | 0.89 | 32% |
| Aggregate misinformation effect | **0.72** | **~22%** |

**Key Finding:** 1 in 5 witnesses will have their memory altered by language alone.

### False Confession Rates

| Dataset | False Confession % |
|---------|-------------------|
| Conservative estimate | 12% |
| Scherr et al. (2020) | ~30% |
| **Garrett (2011) DNA exonerations** | **27 of 250** factually innocent people signed detailed confessions |

### Suggestibility Under Detention

| Condition | Suggestibility Increase |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Anxiety induction | +38% |
| Sleep deprivation | +56% |
| Custody vs neutral setting | +31% (yield), +42% (shift) |
| **Compound detention effect** | **+80–120%** |

## 2.9 Neurodivergent Communication and Systematic Credibility Bias

### The Double Bind

Neurodivergent individuals — those with autism, ADHD, FND, PTSD, CPTSD — present authentically in ways the system is specifically calibrated to misread as deception.

| Neurodivergent Presentation | System Interpretation |
|-----------------------------|----------------------|
| Reduced eye contact (autistic sensory management) | Shifty, untrustworthy |
| Flat affect (autistic/PTSD/FND) | Cold, no remorse, faking |
| Direct communication without hedging | Aggressive, uncooperative |
| Fragmented trauma narrative (PTSD/CPTSD) | Inconsistent, fabricating |
| Variable symptoms (FND) | Faking illness |
| Says "I don't know" when genuinely uncertain | Evasive, hiding something |
| Corrects interviewer's errors | Argumentative |

**The system is not failing neurodivergent people. It is processing them exactly as designed.**

## 2.10 The Behavioral Adaptation Feedback Loop

### Why Heuristics Cannot Work — Regardless

The Signal Inversion Effect creates a paradox that compounds over time:

1. **Authentic behaviour is disbelieved** → Person learns their natural presentation attracts suspicion
2. **Person adopts "honest" behaviours** → Maintains eye contact, speaks fluently, projects confidence
3. **These are the behaviours of actual liars** → Rehearsed, performed, strategically deployed
4. **Detection becomes impossible** → Honest people have been trained to present like deceivers

**The trap is inescapable:** authentic presentation triggers suspicion; performed presentation triggers suspicion; awareness of the problem itself triggers suspicion.

### The System Cannot Self-Correct

This feedback loop explains why the criminal justice system cannot self-correct through experience:

- **True positives are overrepresented in feedback** — confessions are treated as confirmation regardless of truth
- **True negatives are invisible** — when an honest person is believed, no feedback indicates whether belief was warranted
- **False positives are often invisible** — honest people misidentified as deceptive but who don't confess disappear from the data
- **False negatives are impossible to identify** — successful deceivers are, by definition, never identified

## 2.11 Neuroimaging Evidence

### A Note on Scientific Communication

This section includes neuroimaging data and brain images. We do so transparently, citing two reasons:

**Reason 1: The Evidence Is Relevant**
Neuroimaging directly supports the thesis that stress impairs prefrontal cortex function, that autistic individuals process social information differently (not worse), and that the neural signatures of authentic cognitive effort are systematically misread.

**Reason 2: Neuroimaging Information Increases Persuasiveness**
Weisberg et al. (2008) demonstrated that explanations containing neuroscience information are rated as more satisfying, even when that information is logically irrelevant. We include brain images because the data shows they increase belief, and because the underlying evidence is genuinely supportive.

### Figure N1: Stress and Prefrontal Cortex Function

![Stress PFC Figure](neuroimaging_figures/stress_pfc_figure.jpg)

**Source:** Arnsten (2015), *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*. PMC4816215. CC BY.

**Caption:** Changes in brain systems controlling behaviour under conditions of alert safety versus uncontrollable stress. Panel (a) shows the "Alert" state with PFC providing top-down regulation of thought, action, and emotion. Panel (b) shows the "Stressed" state where the PFC "goes offline" and primitive brain circuits (amygdala, basal ganglia, PAG) take over with reflexive/habitual responding.

**Key Quote from Arnsten:**
> "Exposure to uncontrollable stress rapidly evokes chemical changes in brain that impair the higher cognitive functions of the PFC while strengthening primitive brain reactions. This flip from reflective to reflexive brain state may have survival value when we are in danger, but it can be ruinous."

**Application:** A person who has been arrested, stripped, searched, and confined in isolation presents with **measurably reduced prefrontal cortex function**. The neural substrate of rational decision-making has been chemically and functionally degraded. The legal doctrine of "voluntariness" assumes a brain that no longer exists in the detained person.

### Figure N2: Threat Regulatory Neurocircuitry

![Threat Circuitry Figure 1](neuroimaging_figures/threat_circuitry_figure1.jpg)

**Source:** Fenster et al. (2018), *Neuropsychopharmacology*. PMC8617299. CC BY.

**Caption:** Human brain anatomy highlighting regions involved in threat learning, extinction, avoidance, cognitive regulation, and contextual modulation. Key structures: vmPFC (ventromedial prefrontal cortex), dlPFC (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex), amygdala, hippocampus, dACC (dorsal anterior cingulate cortex).

**Application:** These are the neural structures that produce autistic social processing differences, PTSD-related threat dysregulation, and the credibility judgments that observers make. The differences are neurological, not behavioral choices.

### Figure N3: Healthy vs PTSD Threat Circuits

![Threat Circuitry Figure 2](neuroimaging_figures/threat_circuitry_figure2.jpg)

**Source:** Fenster et al. (2018), *Neuropsychopharmacology*. PMC8617299. CC BY.

**Caption:** Panel (A) shows healthy threat circuitry with intact connectivity between dlPFC, vmPFC, hippocampus, and amygdala. Panel (B) shows PTSD-related threat circuitry where "the dlPFC, vmPFC/IL, and hippocampus show impaired functioning with PTSD, whereas the amygdala and dACC/PL are enhanced."

**Application:** PTSD produces measurable changes in the brain circuits responsible for threat processing and memory. The fragmented recall, emotional dysregulation, and avoidant presentation characteristic of trauma are neurological symptoms, not credibility indicators. Observers who interpret these presentations as deception are misreading brain states.

### Neuroimaging Cannot Detect Deception Either

Despite significant research investment, **fMRI-based lie detection does not work reliably enough for forensic use**:

| Study | Claimed Accuracy | Problem |
|-------|------------------|---------|
| Early claims | 90%+ | Not replicated |
| Berkeley 2024 | 79% | Confounded by selfishness |
| 2026 Review | ~70% | "Not suited for use as a lie detector" |

**The brain states associated with "lying" overlap extensively with the brain states associated with being accused, stressed, traumatised, or neurodivergent.**

# Conclusion

A person can be arrested without having done anything wrong. They can be stripped, searched, and confined in a designed environment of institutional control. They can be subjected to interrogation methodology that treats their innocence as deception. They can be tried before a jury that has already convicted them in the media environment. They can be cross-examined in a process that rewrites the memories of witnesses. They can be convicted by twelve people applying inverted heuristics to a prosecution narrative.

And the verdict will say: **guilty**.

The presumption of innocence is not a description of how the criminal justice system operates. It is a **legitimating speech act** — a performative utterance that constitutes, in language, a procedural protection that the institutional architecture consistently overrides in practice.

**The system is not broken. It works exactly as designed.**

To name this gap — between the doctrine and the design, between the words and the world — is the first, necessary, and irreplaceable step toward a criminal justice system that does not merely speak justice but produces it.

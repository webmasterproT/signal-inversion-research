# What This Proves

## What we tested

We mapped the diagnostic features of autism (from the DSM-5 — the manual doctors use) against the behaviours the public believes indicate lying (from a survey of 11,227 people across 75 countries). Then we ran 100,000 simulations to calculate how much harder it is for an autistic person to be believed.

## What we found

The overlap is nearly perfect. The things autistic people do naturally — less eye contact, flat tone of voice, fidgeting, delayed responses, different narrative style — are almost exactly the things the public thinks mean someone is lying. And as Study 3 showed, those beliefs are 74% wrong.

An autistic person telling the truth triggers **3.5x more** false deception alarms than a neurotypical person in the same situation. The compound credibility penalty is d = 3.18 (anything above 0.8 is "large" — this is off the charts).

But here's the critical part: **Maras et al. (2019)** showed that when mock jurors were told a witness was autistic, the credibility penalty disappeared. When they weren't told, autistic witnesses were rated less believable. Same person, same testimony, same truth.

**It's not the person. It's the framework.**

## Why it matters

Autistic people are overrepresented in forensic populations (2-18% vs 1-2% in the general population). Their natural behaviour — the way their neurology works — makes them look guilty to a system using cues that are empirically wrong. They can't stop making eye contact differently. They can't stop their voice being flat. And every one of those involuntary behaviours triggers a "liar" alarm in observers.

Only 37% of police officers have received any autism training. The rest are reading autism as deception.

## The numbers (simplified)

- **7 cues** modelled (eye contact, fidgeting, disfluency, flat affect, anxiety, response time, detail level)
- **74%** of those cues are empirically inverted (they indicate truth, not lies)
- **3.5x** — autistic truth-tellers trigger 3.5 times more false deception alarms
- **d = 3.18** — compound credibility penalty (massive)
- **100,000** simulations
- **Disclosure reverses it** — tell the jury the person is autistic and the bias disappears

## So what?

If you're autistic, the justice system reads your neurology as guilt. Not because you did anything. Because the instrument is calibrated to a brain that isn't yours. And nobody told the jury.

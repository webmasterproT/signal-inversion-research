# What This Proves

## What we tested

Using US Census data (American Community Survey 2022, California), we asked: what predicts whether someone gets a college degree — their ancestry (a proxy for genetics) or the language they speak at home (a proxy for environment)?

We ran three models: ancestry alone, language alone, and both together. Then we did the critical test: take people of the **same ancestry** but different language environments, and see if their outcomes differ.

## What we found

Language (environment) explains more variance in educational outcomes than ancestry (genetics). Within the same ancestry group — same genetic background — English speakers have significantly higher college attainment than non-English speakers. The gap is large and statistically significant across Hispanic/Latino, Asian, and European ancestry groups.

The generational analysis seals it: US-born individuals converge toward similar outcomes regardless of ancestry. Give people the same environment, and their outcomes converge. Same DNA, different postcode, different life.

When you add language to an ancestry-only model, it adds significant predictive power. When you add ancestry to a language-only model, it adds less. The environment variable is doing more of the work.

## Why it matters

This is the "same person, different postcode" argument with real numbers. The system treats people as if their outcomes reflect their character — their choices, their effort, their nature. The data says otherwise. Your environment — where you grew up, what language was spoken around you, what opportunities existed in your postcode — predicts your outcomes better than anything about you as a person.

This matters for every system that punishes individuals for outcomes that were environmentally produced: criminal justice, welfare, education, housing.

## The numbers (simplified)

- **~390,000** individuals in the analysis (California census)
- Language alone explains **more variance** than ancestry alone
- Within the same ancestry group, English speakers have **significantly higher** college rates
- US-born individuals across ancestries **converge** toward similar outcomes
- All key results p < .001

## So what?

Your postcode is a better predictor of your life than your DNA. We built entire systems — courts, prisons, welfare offices — on the assumption it's the other way around. The data says those systems are punishing people for where they were born.

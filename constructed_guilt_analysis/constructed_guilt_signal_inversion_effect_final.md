                                The Signal Inversion Effect
How Authentic Cognition Mimics Deception: Evidence from Trial Testimony, Criminal Confessions,
                                 and Global Belief Systems

                                    Working Paper — Preprint — March 2026


Abstract
       This paper presents converging evidence for the Signal Inversion Effect: a systematic pattern
       whereby authentic cognitive and linguistic behaviours associated with truthfulness are misidentified
       as indicators of deception by human observers. Across four studies we find: (1) disfluency is
       significantly higher in truthful trial testimony than deceptive testimony (d = 0.60, p = .004), a
       medium effect 6× larger than the median deception cue; (2) false confessions show massive linguistic
       distancing, with impersonal pronoun contractions up to 12× higher and second-person references
       7.6× higher than true confessions; (3) 91.3% of publicly believed deception cues are empirically
       unrelated or inversely related to actual deception (21/23 cues inverted; binomial test p < .0001;
       weighted inversion index = 0.81); and (4) human deception detection averages 54% accuracy (lie
       detection specifically: 47%, below chance), while algorithmic classification using linguistic features
       achieves 63.5–83%. Demeanour-based credibility assessment does not merely fail to detect
       deception — it systematically penalises truthful speakers whose authentic cognitive effort produces
       the signals misread as guilt.

       Keywords: deception detection, credibility assessment, signal inversion, disfluency, false
       confessions, belief-reality inversion, demeanour evidence


1. Introduction
Can humans reliably determine whether someone is telling the truth? Bond and DePaulo (2006), synthesising
206 studies involving 24,483 judges, found that people achieve an average accuracy of 54% — barely above
chance. Lie detection specifically was 47%, meaning observers are more likely to believe a liar than to
identify the deception. Professional training does not help: police officers (∼55%), judges (∼54%), and
customs officials (∼55%) perform no better than the untrained public (Bond & DePaulo, 2006; Vrij, Granhag,
& Porter, 2010).
   This paper argues that the failure is not merely insufficient skill but a fundamental directional inversion.
The behaviours people believe indicate deception — gaze aversion, disfluency, nervous appearance, hedging
— are either empirically unrelated to deception or more characteristic of truthful speech. The smooth,
confident delivery interpreted as honesty is more consistent with rehearsed or deceptive communication. We
present evidence across four converging studies.

2. Study 1: Linguistic Markers in Trial Testimony

2.1 Method
We analysed 121 video transcripts from the Real-Life Trial Dataset (Pérez-Rosas et al., 2015): 60 truthful and
61 deceptive clips from actual court proceedings, with ground truth established through verdicts and
post-conviction exonerations. Eight linguistic variables were coded per 100 words. Non-parametric group
comparisons (Mann-Whitney U) were used due to non-normal distributions.

3. Study 2: True vs. False Confessions

3.1 Method
We reanalysed published data from Rizzelli, Kassin, and Gales (2021): 37 confessions proven false through
DNA exoneration (Innocence Project) and 98 confessions presumed true (FBI case files). Per-confession word
rates were computed from Appendix A raw counts and tested with chi-square.

4. Study 3: The Belief–Reality Inversion Matrix

4.1 Method
We systematically matched deception cues appearing in both the Global Deception Research Team (GDRT,
2006) survey of 11,227 participants across 75 countries and DePaulo et al.'s (2003) meta-analysis, yielding 23
matched cues. For each, we coded whether the publicly believed direction matched or was inverted relative to
the empirical evidence. We supplemented DePaulo effect sizes with our Study 1 disfluency finding.

4.2 Results

belief endorsement rates.




Of 23 matched cues, 21 (91.3%) were inverted — the public believes these cues indicate deception, but
empirical evidence shows either no relationship or the opposite direction. Only 2 cues (appearing nervous,

6. General Discussion
The four studies converge on a single finding: the human credibility assessment system operates with an
inverted compass. The single most believed deception cue worldwide (gaze aversion, 63.7%) has effectively
zero empirical validity (d = 0.05). Meanwhile, disfluency — interpreted by observers as evasion — shows a

---
robust association with truthfulness (d = 0.60). The inversion is not marginal: 91% of matched cues are wrong
(p < .0001), and the weighted index shows the strongest beliefs are the most wrong.

6.1 Implications for Legal Proceedings
When judges, jurors, or investigators assess demeanour, they draw on heuristics that are empirically inverted.
A witness who pauses, says 'um,' breaks eye contact to search memory, qualifies statements with 'I think,' and
produces a fragmented narrative is exhibiting cognitive signatures of genuine recall. These same behaviours
activate the folk model of deception, producing credibility judgments that are directionally wrong. This cannot
be solved through training: Bond and DePaulo (2006) found professional groups perform no better than
untrained members of the public.

6.2 The Behavioural Adaptation Feedback Loop
Individuals repeatedly disbelieved despite telling the truth — because their authentic speech patterns trigger
inverted credibility heuristics — may learn to suppress authentic patterns and adopt performed behaviours
(steady eye contact, fluent delivery, reduced hedging) matching observers' expectations. This means repeated
demeanour-based credibility assessment does not merely fail; it actively trains truthful speakers to
communicate in ways that resemble deceptive speech, eroding the very cues that might otherwise assist
veridical assessment. The system is self-defeating.

6.3 The Unpredictable 9%
Two cues showed alignment between belief and reality: appearing nervous (d = 0.27) and negative statements
(d = 0.21). However, even these small effects are insufficient for reliable individual assessment, and their
presence in a sea of inverted cues creates a dangerous illusion: because occasionally a nervousness-based
judgment happens to be correct, observers are reinforced in their broader reliance on a framework that is 91%
wrong. The small kernel of truth sustains the larger inversion.

6.4 The Compounding Effect: Autism Spectrum and Credibility Assessment
The Signal Inversion Effect is not uniformly distributed across populations. Individuals on the autism
spectrum are disproportionately affected because the behavioural characteristics of ASD overlap substantially
with the very cues that trigger inverted credibility judgments. Gaze aversion, the #1 believed deception cue
worldwide (63.7% endorsement, d = 0.05), is also a core feature of autistic social communication. Repetitive
movements and fidgeting (#3 believed cue, 47.2%, d = -0.01) form part of the diagnostic criteria for ASD.
Atypical speech patterns, flat affect, and reduced reciprocity — all characteristic of autism — activate the
same folk-deception heuristics that our Study 3 demonstrates are 91% wrong.
   Lim, Young, and Brewer (2022) tested this directly. In a study of 1,410 observers rating video interviews
of 30 autistic and 29 neurotypical individuals, autistic adults were perceived as more deceptive and less
credible than neurotypical adults when telling the truth. This effect was not driven by any single
behaviour but by overall presentation — suggesting that the accumulated impact of multiple autism-typical
behaviours, each individually triggering inverted deception heuristics, produces a compounding credibility
penalty that cannot be attributed to any specific cue.
    Bagnall et al. (2023) extended these findings to police suspect interviews. Innocent autistic mock-suspects
reported fewer details supporting their innocence than non-autistic suspects, experienced greater difficulty
understanding questions, reported higher anxiety, and perceived the interview as less supportive. These are not
indicators of deception; they are predictable consequences of autistic cognitive and communication profiles
interacting with the social demands of an interrogative context. Yet each of these responses — reduced detail,
visible anxiety, communication difficulty — maps directly onto the inverted cues our analysis identifies.
    The implications are stark. An autistic individual in a police interaction or courtroom who exhibits genuine
distress, averts gaze to manage sensory overload, produces disfluent speech under cognitive load, and

---
struggles to provide a smooth narrative is not exhibiting deceptive behaviour. They are exhibiting the
neurological signatures of autism interacting with a high-stress social environment. When these
behaviours are interpreted through the lens of credibility heuristics that are 91% empirically wrong, the result
is a systematic miscarriage of assessment.
    Critically, Maras et al. (2019) found that disclosing an autism diagnosis and providing information about
autism to mock jurors reversed the credibility penalty: autistic witnesses were then rated as slightly more
credible than neurotypical witnesses. This finding suggests that the bias is not intractable but is contingent on
the observer’s interpretive framework. When observers are given a correct framework (this person’s
behaviour reflects autism, not deception), their judgments improve. Without that framework, the Signal
Inversion Effect applies with compounding force.

6.5 Limitations
Study 1 uses a small corpus (N = 121) with short clips. Study 2 uses aggregate counts rather than
individual-level data. Study 3 requires judgment in matching belief categories to meta-analytic cues. These
limitations notwithstanding, convergence across independent datasets, methodologies, and research groups
strengthens confidence in the general pattern.

6.6 Conclusion
Demeanour-based credibility assessment is not merely unreliable. It is systematically biased against truthful
communication. The cues authentic cognitive effort produces during genuine recall — disfluency, hedging,
fragmented narrative, gaze aversion during memory search — are precisely the cues human observers
interpret as deception. This Signal Inversion Effect operates across trial testimony, criminal confessions, and
global belief systems, and is resistant to professional training. Until credibility assessment is reformed to
eliminate reliance on demeanour evidence, truthful individuals will continue to be structurally disadvantaged
in any context where honesty is judged by appearance rather than content.
